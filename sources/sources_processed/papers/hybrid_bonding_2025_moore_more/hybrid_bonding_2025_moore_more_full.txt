Cu–Cu hybrid bonding technology: from physical mechanisms 
to system integration for 3D ICs
Bing Jiang1 · Yutong Wu1 · Zhixiang Zhong1 · Kangkai Fan1 · Qiubao Lin2 · Jianbo Liang3 · Hezhou Liu1 · Xinke Liu1

Abstract
With the rapid development of microelectronic technology toward high-performance and high-density integration, traditional 
bonding methods face dual challenges of interconnection density and bonding temperature. The Cu–Cu hybrid bonding, 
leveraging its outstanding benefits in sub-micron interconnection and efficient thermal management, has emerged as an 
innovative solution to overcome the limitations of traditional packaging. This paper systematically explores the physical 
mechanisms, integration technologies, key process breakthroughs, and macroscopic applications of Cu–Cu hybrid bonding. 
The study highlights the revolutionary advantages of hybrid bonding, such as ultra-fine vertical interconnection spacing. A 
detailed analysis of two major physical mechanisms in key technical pathways is presented. In practical applications, Cu–Cu 
hybrid bonding has demonstrated success in high-bandwidth memory, three-dimensional heterogeneous integration, and 
complementary metal–oxide–semiconductor image sensors. A notable example is Sony’s Cu/SiO₂-bonded image sensor, 
markedly improving imaging quality and integration density. However, challenges such as thermal boundary resistance 
control, process complexity, and large-scale production costs remain barriers to industrialization. Future advancements, 
including novel passivation materials and integration with ultra-wide bandgap semiconductors, promise to further propel 
the performance and reliability of radio frequency (RF) devices, power electronics, and artificial intelligence chips. This 
review provides a comprehensive framework for both theoretical exploration and practical implementation of Cu–Cu hybrid 
bonding technology.
Keywords  Cu–Cu hybrid bonding · 3D integration · Low-temperature bonding · Interfacial reliability · Physical 
mechanisms
1  Introduction
With the rapid development of microelectronics technol-
ogy, the demand for high-performance, high-density inte-
grated electronic devices is increasing [1–4]. Against this 
backdrop, Cu–Cu hybrid bonding (HB) technology, as an 
advanced interconnect technology, has attracted widespread 
attention due to its excellent electrical conductivity, ther-
mal conductivity, and good mechanical stability. In recent 
years, the rapid development of emerging technologies such 
as artificial intelligence and autonomous driving [5, 6] has 
brought unprecedented challenges and opportunities to the 
packaging and integration of electronic devices [7]. These 
technologies not only demand higher performance and lower 
power consumption from electronic devices but also require 
more complex functional integration within smaller form 
factors. For instance, artificial intelligence (AI) chips need to 
process massive amounts of data [8–11], placing extremely 
high demands on parallel processing capabilities and ther-
mal management. Meanwhile, autonomous driving systems 
rely on the fusion of multiple sensors and real-time data 
transmission, making interconnect density and reliability 
between chips critical factors. Therefore, traditional pack-
aging and integration technologies are no longer sufficient

Cu–Cu HB technology not only enables high-density 
interconnects between chips but also offers higher reliabil-
ity and lower resistance compared to traditional soldering 
techniques, which is crucial for HPC, high-speed commu-
nication, and advanced packaging technologies [16]. Cu–Cu 
HB combines the advantages of metal bonding and dielectric 
bonding, achieving both electrical and mechanical connec-
tions by forming stable chemical bonds or physical con-
nections on the surface of metallic Cu. The advantages of 
Cu–Cu HB include the following: fine-pitch interconnects, 
enabling sub-micron-level interconnects to increase chip 
density and performance; lower parasitic capacitance and 
resistance, reducing signal transmission delays and power 
consumption; and thinner packaging thickness. Compared 
to traditional flip-chip bonding using solder bumps, Cu–Cu 
HB can achieve a thinner packaging profile with gapless 
connections between chips.
The development of this technology has not only pro-
pelled the progress of chip-scale packaging and 3D inte-
grated circuit (3D-IC) technologies but has also highlighted 
the importance of thermocompression bonding (TCB) tech-
nology. TCB plays a crucial role in achieving the vertical 
stacking of chips or wafers within 3D integration, offering 
higher input/output (I/O) density [17, 18] compared to tradi-
tional soldering methods. Among various TCB techniques, 
copper-to-copper bonding is favored for its remarkable 
electrical and thermal qualities, high mechanical stability 
in bonding sections, and compatibility and cost-effective-
ness in packaging manufacturing procedures. However, HB 
technology faces numerous challenges in its development. 
The most critical issue is the significant difference in bond-
ing mechanisms between metal-to-metal and dielectric-to-
dielectric bonds, making it imperative to develop a bonding 
technology that can accommodate both. In addition, most 
existing bonding processes rely on high-temperature envi-
ronments, which can cause thermal damage to devices and 
trigger a series of thermally related issues. Therefore, devel-
oping efficient thermal management solutions to mitigate the 
negative impact of high temperatures on device performance 
and reliability has become an important issue that needs 
to be addressed urgently. As device performance continues 
to advance in accordance with Moore’s law [19], advanced 
packaging technologies, such as 3D stacking and interposer 
technology, have become crucial for achieving HPC. These 
technologies enable smaller form factors, lower internal 
latency, and reduced power consumption for inter-chip 
communication through vertical high-density interconnects. 
Meanwhile, interposer technology facilitates heterogeneous 
integration of different technologies and materials. In 3D 
stacking [20, 21] and interposer interconnects, solder-based 
bumping technology [22] is currently commonly used for 
assembly. However, when the packaging size is reduced 
to ≤ 50 μm, solder-based bumping technology faces numer-
ous challenges [23, 24], such as solder extrusion, bridg-
ing, and the formation of intermetallic compounds (IMCs). 
Therefore, eliminating solder has become critically impor-
tant, which is the key context for introducing Cu–Cu direct 
bonding technology.

technologies, and application domains, with a focus on elu-

ous factors within this field. Section 2 primarily addresses 
the physical mechanisms, aiming to explore the advantages, 
potential, and challenges of Cu–Cu HB technology in power 
device applications. Section 3 delves into the critical break-

can broaden the applicability of Cu–Cu HB technology 
across diverse fields. Section 4 discusses the current and 
emerging application areas of Cu–Cu HB, highlighting both 
existing implementations and future opportunities.
Through this study, we propose novel pathways to 
advance Cu–Cu HB technology, with the goal of fostering 
its widespread adoption in next-generation power electronic 
devices while enhancing their performance and reliability.

Cu–Cu metal bonding and dielectric–dielectric bonding, 
typically oxide–oxide bonding. This technology embeds 
metal contacts within dielectric materials and utilizes heat 
treatment to bond the two materials together. By leveraging

2  Traditional bonding faces dual challenges
To utilize these chips for their intended purposes, wafers 
need to be diced into individual chips and then connected 
externally to enable power supply. The method used to con-
nect wires (the pathways for electrical signals) is called wire 
bonding. In fact, the use of metal wires to connect circuits 
is a very traditional approach, and its usage is gradually 
declining. Recently, flip-chip bonding and through-silicon 
via (TSV) [25] have emerged as the new mainstream meth-
ods. Flip-chip bonding, also known as bump bonding [26], is 
a method that uses solder balls [27] (small bumps) for bond-
ing. TSV, on the other hand, represents a more advanced 
approach. To understand the fundamental concepts of bond-
ing, we will first explore wire bonding [28], which is the 
traditional method. There are three main methods for con-
necting metal wires to bonding pads as follows: the ther-
mocompression method [29], which involves heating and 
compressing the bonding pad and the capillary tool (a tool 
similar to a capillary tube that moves metal wires) to form 
a connection; the ultrasonic method [30], which does not 
involve heating, and instead, ultrasonic waves are applied to 
the capillary tool to create the connection; the thermionic 
method, which is a hybrid approach that combines both heat-
ing and ultrasonic waves. It has long been the mainstream 
solution in the field of semiconductor packaging. These tech-
nologies enable the interconnection of chips to substrates 
or chips through metal solder joints, which rely on high-
temperature and high-pressure processes to complete the 
interface bonding. However, as Moore’s law approaches its 
physical limits, the requirements for high-density intercon-
nects, low power consumption, and heterogeneous integra-
tion of ICs become more stringent, and the limitations of 
traditional bonding technologies become apparent.
The primary limitation of traditional bonding technology 
is its limited integration density, which makes it difficult to 
meet the requirements of advanced semiconductor devices 
for the co-packaging of heterogeneous material systems. At 
the same time, the thermal management issues are addressed 
through the integration of diamond/copper microchannels 
to achieve this goal. Lu et al. [31] investigated the ther-
mal transfer and fluid characteristics of diamond/copper

two principal pathways: the first adheres to the core tenets

this framework, 3D heterogeneous integration emerges as a 
pivotal breakthrough, primarily achieved through two imple-

Modern electronic packaging confronts critical thermal 
management challenges in advanced microelectronics. The 
persistent escalation of integration density has revealed 
inherent limitations in conventional interconnection tech-
nologies under elevated thermal conditions. Current bond-
ing methodologies exhibit substantial deficiencies in thermal 
stability, particularly manifesting as substrate deformation 
induced by thermal gradients during thermocompression 
processes [38]. When processing temperatures exceed criti-
cal thresholds (typically 350–400 °C for silicon-based sys-
tems), plastic deformation and interconnect misalignment 
in silicon wafers become prevalent, primarily attributed to 
anisotropic thermal expansion coefficients of constituent 
materials [39]. Such thermally induced distortions not only 
compromise interfacial coplanarity but also precipitate reli-
ability concerns including threshold voltage drift and con-
tact resistance degradation. Figure 3 illustrates: (i) standard 
bonding process flow; (ii) interfacial stress concentration 
from thermal compression; and (iii) surface activation treat-
ment enabling 50–70 °C reduction in Cu–Cu bonding tem-
perature thresholds.
Thermomechanical reliability emerges as a paramount 
concern in 3D integration architectures. While TSV tech-
nology demonstrates superior miniaturization and func-
tional integration capabilities, its structural configuration 
inherently exacerbates thermal stress challenges. The TSV 
fabrication sequence—encompassing deep reactive ion etch-
ing, copper electroplating, and wafer thinning—introduces 
significant thermomechanical mismatch between copper 
interconnects (coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE): 
17 ppm/°C) and silicon substrates (CTE: 2.6 ppm/°C). This 
CTE disparity generates interfacial shear stresses exceeding 
80 MPa under thermal cycling conditions (ΔT = 150 °C) for 
50 μm-diameter TSVs [40, 41], precipitating four primary 
failure mechanisms: (i) stress-modulated carrier mobil-
ity reduction (25–40% degradation) causing signal distor-
tion and propagation delay; (ii) metal–dielectric interfacial 
delamination leading to dielectric leakage currents > 10 μA/
cm2; (iii) accelerated electromigration rates (Black’s equa-
tion exponent n = 2.1–2.5) degrading interconnect longevity; 
(iv) stress concentration-induced microcrack propagation

In addition, we have studied the bond and temperature 
of bonding technology in recent years. The optimization 
of process temperature and the improvement of interfacial 
adhesion have become the focus of research in the field 
of microelectronic packaging. Figure 4 shows that the hot 
pressing temperature of advanced bonding technology has 
shown a significant downward trend in recent years. The 
comparison table shows that there are significant differences 
in the thermodynamic properties of wire bonding, hot press 
bonding, and Cu–Cu HB: although wire bonding can achieve 
low-temperature bonding at 200–260 °C, the interfacial 
shear strength is usually less than 100 MPa; the hot-pressed

°C; the surface-modified Cu–Cu HB technology successfully 
reduced the process temperature to −250 °C at room tem-

Therefore, it is necessary to study low-temperature HB.
The transition to HB, compared to bump bonding, is 
driven by a relatively straightforward reason. Both 3D 
memory stacking and heterogeneous integration (key play-
ers in the “More than Moore” era) require extremely high 
interconnect densities. As mentioned, HB can meet this 
demand. Compared to micro-bumps, which inherently sup-
port high-density interconnect schemes, HB offers smaller 
I/O terminals and reduced pitch interconnects. The spacing 
between chips depends on the height of micro-bumps, but in 
HB, this distance is nearly zero. As a result, HB interconnect 
schemes can significantly reduce the overall package thick-
ness, potentially saving hundreds of micrometers in multi-
chip stacked packages.
3  The type of process and physical 
mechanism
3.1  DBI (direct bond interconnect) hybrid bonding
The direct oxide bonding process [49] typically first con-
tacts the wafers at room temperature and then forms strong 
covalent bonds. In traditional preparation, the surface of the 
silicon wafer is mainly in a hydroxyl termination state after 
water washing. In order to obtain a high-strength bonding 
interface, it is necessary to promote the formation of hydro-
gen bonds between hydroxyl groups through heat treatment 
and initiate a polycondensation reaction, and finally com-
plete the conversion of silicon–oxygen covalent bonds in 
the annealing stage. This mechanism, known as hydrophilic 
bonding, has the significant advantage of exhibiting good 
compatibility with both silica and copper metal. Experi-
ments show that under the condition of low-temperature 
annealing at about 300 °C, two hydrophilic-treated surfaces 
can form a defect-free high-strength bonding layer. Com-
bined with the chemical mechanical planarization process,

3.2  SAB (surface‑activated bonding)‑based hybrid 
bonding
The surface of the metal can activate the spontaneous 
bonding characteristics (surface-activated bonding (SAB)) 
through ion beam irradiation. This process effectively 
removes the oxidation layer mechanism, allowing the bond-
ing process to be completed without high-temperature treat-
ment. This low-temperature bonding technology breaks the 
traditional thermal budget limitations and provides critical 
process support for the manufacturing of 3D integrated 
devices [51]. The low-temperature mixed bonding process 
route has garnered significant attention due to its applica-
tion potential in temperature environments below 200 °C; 
however, actual engineering implementation still faces con-
siderable challenges. Research indicates that the vacuum-
processing stage has become the primary technical bottle-
neck in achieving the surface activation bonding technology 
[52]. Although vacuum environment operation has become a 
standard process in semiconductor manufacturing, the chal-
lenges of precise alignment remain unresolved. The existing 
bonding equipment exhibits a significant gap in positioning 
accuracy under vacuum conditions compared to atmospheric 
pressure. The thermal cycling that accompanies the switch-
ing of atmospheric pressure can easily lead to mechanical 
structural deformation, primarily due to the material stress 
response under alternating vacuum-atmospheric conditions. 
The core advantage of this technology lies in its ground-
breaking non-chemical removal mechanism for the copper 
oxide layer, allowing the bonding process to be completed 
without relying on traditional hydrophilic pretreatment, 
thereby making high-reliability bonding achievable under 
ambient temperature conditions. Renaud et al. [53] success-
fully demonstrated for the first time Cu–oxide direct HB 
at low temperatures (150 °C) and even room temperature.

3.3  Cu–Cu bonding physical mechanisms
Conventional copper interconnects predominantly employ 
TCB, where applied external pressure combined with 
controlled thermal exposure facilitates interfacial copper 
interdiffusion to establish metallic bonds. Jang et al. [54] 
systematically investigated bonding efficacy under 25 kN 
compressive load across temperature gradients (300 °C, 
350 °C, 400 °C). Characterization results demonstrate that 
elevating process temperature to 400 °C achieves an optimal 
interfacial bonding energy of 4.93 J/m2, accompanied by 
complete metallurgical integration verified through trans-
mission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis. Nevertheless, 
thermomechanical stress accumulation during thermal ramp-
ing phases, coupled with prolonged high-temperature expo-
sure, induces substrate warpage (measured curvature > 2.5 
μm/mm) and microcrack propagation (crack density ~ ­103 
­cm−2). These phenomena collectively precipitate interfa-
cial delamination risks, potentially leading to functional 
impairment in advanced packaging architectures. Mean-
while, high-temperature processes also place more stringent 
demands on equipment performance. To avoid excessive 
deformation or damage to chips caused by high tempera-
tures, low-temperature Cu–Cu bonding technology becomes 
particularly important.
To address current technological bottlenecks, in-depth 
investigation of the fundamental physics of the mechanisms 
of Cu–Cu HB becomes imperative. This study focuses on 
the synergistic interactions between dielectric and metal-
lic layers during HB. The ­SiO2–SiO2 bonding process can 
be categorized into hydrophilic and hydrophobic variants, 
depending on the surface morphology of the ­SiO2 interfaces. 
Hydrophilic bonding is achieved by forming –OH groups 
on ­SiO2 surfaces, typically through pretreatment using SC1 
­(NH4OH/H2O2/H2O) or SPM ­(H2SO4/H2O2) solutions. 
The mechanism of hydrophilic bonding primarily relies on 
condensation reactions between hydroxyl groups (–OH) on 
mirror-polished silicon wafers [55]. As illustrated in Fig. 6, 
Tong and Gösele [56] proposed a four-stage mechanistic

the fundamental process involved in achieving Cu–Cu HB.

After the dielectric layer bonding is realized, the second 
step mainly involves the process of direct Cu–Cu bonding. 
Metal interconnects are usually formed spontaneously in

copper structure to expand radially when heated, resulting

bonding in recent years, as well as the comparison of bond-

at 400 °C but faces two major issues: first, high temperatures 
can easily induce copper diffusion, leading to device failure; 
second, the bond strength achieved solely through thermo-
compression is relatively low and insufficient to meet appli-
cation requirements. Therefore, researchers have begun to 
explore low-temperature bonding processes. Although many 
laboratory techniques can achieve low-temperature bonding, 
most are not suitable for large-scale production. For exam-
ple, methods such as preparing HD-Cu or nanocrystalline 
copper to enhance copper diffusion are not only complex 
and costly but also unsuitable for mass production. Pillar-
concave bonding can quickly achieve low-temperature bond-
ing but requires extremely high pressure, which poses high 
demands on equipment and is prone to damage. The method 
of injecting formic acid vapor can bond quickly but faces 
challenges in gas flow control and high equipment require-
ments during actual production. Methods such as passiva-
tion layers, self-assembled monolayers, and indirect bond-
ing introduce additional materials and complex processes, 
resulting in high costs and low yields. Ultrasonic bonding, 
although capable of achieving low-temperature bonding in 
a short time, demands high surface flatness of the wafer and 
is complicated by the high hardness and rapid oxidation rate 
of copper, which increase the bonding difficulty.
4.2  Low‑temperature Cu/SiO2 hybrid bonding 
technology
For fine-pitch applications, bump-free Cu/SiO₂ intercon-
nects are the most promising HB structures, achievable 
through DBI or hot-pressed bonding (TCB) techniques. This 
technology was developed by Ziptronix and is suitable for 
scalable HB processes [49]. The results show that DBI can 
reduce the interfacial parasitic capacitance by 80% in the 
spacing range below 20 μm compared with the traditional 
bumping process. It has been further demonstrated that the 
interconnect pitch can be scaled to less than 10 μm [70, 71], 
and the low-temperature HB process consists of two key 
steps, as shown in Fig. 9a, b. According to the analysis of 
the physical mechanism above, the direct bonding between 
SiO₂ at room temperature can be realized by hydrophilic 
modification of the surface. The copper–inter–copper bond-
ing is then completed through a batch annealing method at 
temperatures ranging from 200 °C to 400 °C. The annealing 
heating rate should be limited to less than 5 °C/min to avoid 
microcrack defects at the edge of the copper pillar. Although 
this method has high production efficiency, the interface 
quality of SiO₂ is easily affected by both residual moisture 
and copper thermal expansion stress during annealing [72].
Additionally, another nanotwin Cu structure for potential 
Cu/SiO2 HB utilizes the excellent diffusion traits of (111) 
crystalline copper, and the nanotwin copper film can be 
effectively bonded after 1 h of treatment at 200 °C. Based on 
this property, Chiu et al. [73] developed a composite struc-
ture of nanotwin copper and silica for low-temperature HB 
systems. Three morphologies were prepared and analyzed: 
nanotwin copper full-coverage films, and disc-shaped and 
pad-like hybrid structures (see Fig. 9c–j). SAT and scan-
ning electron microscopy (SEM) characterization confirmed 
that the nanotwin copper film with 99% (111) preferential 
orientation can complete high-quality bonding at 250 °C.

4.3  Wafer‑level structure GaN/Si heterogeneous

Ren et al. [74] innovatively combined high-voltage gallium 
nitride (GaN) high electron mobility transistor (HEMT)

and co-gate topology, and used device-level co-optimization 
to significantly suppress the parasitic effects of intercon-
nect. However, this architecture requires a redesign of the 
process integration, such as ion implantation and annealing 
steps in the CMOS source-drain region after the comple-
tion of the GaN epitaxy process, so as to reduce the effect 
of high-temperature heat treatment on the dopant diffusion 
depth and concentration gradient. Limited by the integration 
complexity and density constraints of GaN devices, and the 
fact that HEMT devices can only realize N-type transistor 
functions, the device type is simplified, and it is difficult to 
meet the design requirements of ICs. On the other hand, the 
compatibility of high-voltage and low-voltage components 
in the power system needs to be solved urgently.
In response to the above challenges, Du et  al. [75]

through the graphical selective epitaxy technology, the 
problem of GaN/Si-based P-well CMOS process matching 
is innovatively solved, and the platform-based device archi-
tecture is constructed, which breaks through the multi-level 
technical bottleneck in the integration of large-size silicon-
based CMOS and GaN chips, and successfully develops 
core components such as high-performance bulk silicon PN 
diodes, bipolar transistors (PNP/NPN), and AlGaN/GaN 
HEMT. By solving the key problems such as resistance and

parasitic effect and stress coupling mechanism, a new 
path is opened up for the design of gallium nitride ICs. The 
research team also built a heterogeneous integrated tech-
nology verification platform that integrates silicon-based 
CMOS and gallium nitride processes.

4.4  Other integration technologies
At present, a three-dimensional multilayer bonding tech-
nology using Cu–Cu hybrid wafer bonding has also been 
developed. By applying robust Cu–Cu bonding in conjunc-
tion with the TSV process, a wafer-level multi-stacking 
structure was successfully demonstrated. This achievement 
relies on a comprehensive understanding of the bonding 
interface, TSV, and edge engineering to avoid defects and 
ensure high yields. Chemical mechanical polishing (CMP) 
and surface treatment significantly impact the Cu/dielec-
tric HB surface, particularly the planarization of the wafer 
back, which is a critical technology for subsequent robust 
bonding. Moreover, improvements in etching during TSV 
formation and considerations for integrated edge process-
ing have optimized engineering aspects, enhancing bond-
ing quality and reproducibility, leading to a high yield of 
electrical connections, including those at the wafer edges. 
This technology can be applied in various fields, includ-
ing sensors, memory devices, logic devices, and their 
combinations.
5  Breakthroughs in hybrid bonding 
technology
In Cu–Cu HB manufacturing, the control of the bonding 
environment is one of the core challenges, and the key to 
this is to achieve a flat and contamination-free dielectric 
surface through CMP [77]. In order to reduce the risk of 
crystal shape and warpage during the bonding process, it is 
important to develop dielectric materials that are resistant to 
short-time annealing at low temperatures, as they need to be 
compatible with lower annealing temperatures and shorter 
process cycles. In addition, optimization of the ECD process 
can improve the overall bonding efficiency by adjusting the 
uniformity of the copper filling (e.g., inhibiting the forma-
tion of microvoids) and reducing the thermal budget required 
for annealing.

6  Current application domains
As semiconductor devices continue to evolve to higher den-
sity, lower power consumption, and heterogeneous integra-
tion, advanced packaging technologies (such as 3D ICs and 
chiplet architectures) have become one of the core paths to 
break through the bottleneck of Moore’s law. Under this 
trend, Cu–Cu HB has become a key technology for high-
bandwidth and low-latency chip system integration due to 
its ultra-high interconnect density (<1 μm pitch), excellent 
electrothermal performance, and heterogeneous material 
compatibility. Especially in the fields of 5G communica-
tions, HPC, and AI chips, Cu–Cu HB significantly reduces 
parasitic resistance (<10 mΩ) and thermal resistance by 
replacing traditional solder bumps with direct copper inter-
connects, providing physical support for the co-optimization 
of heterogeneous chips such as logic cells, memories, and 
optoelectronic devices.
6.1  Application fields
6.1.1  High‑bandwidth memory (HBM)
Cu–Cu hybrid bonding technology is actively developed 
and adopted in HBM applications. By stacking multiple 
memory chips and bonding them through Cu–Cu HB, high-
bandwidth and low-latency memory access is achieved.
6.1.2  3D chip integration
Chips with different functions, such as processors, caches, 
and logic chips, are vertically stacked and interconnected 
through Cu–Cu HB. This allows for more functional inte-
gration within limited space, enhancing the overall system 
performance and energy efficiency.
6.1.3  CMOS image sensors
Cu–Cu HB technology is used to bond pixel array chips with 
signal-processing chips, resulting in a more compact sensor 
structure. This improves image quality and processing speed 
while facilitating the miniaturization of camera modules.
Sony was the first to apply HB technology to contact 
image sensor (CIS), addressing the challenges of high-den-
sity pixels and electrical signal processing. In 2016, Sony 
launched the IMX260 image sensor based on Cu/SiO₂ HB 
technology [80], which was used in the Samsung Galaxy 
S7. The process included wafer activation, pre-bonding, two 
annealing steps, and high-temperature bonding [81], achiev-
ing excellent electrical reliability and image performance. In 
the past 2 years, Sony has reduced the interconnect pitch to 
below 2 μm [82], driving the miniaturization and high-per-
formance of CIS image sensors. Figure 12 shows Sony’s BI-
SIC HB process flow: (i) TSV-free 3D CIS and processor IC 
integration (HB); (ii) SEM image of a 3D CIS mixed bonded 
to a processor IC; (iii) three-chip stacked (HB) structure. In 
2018, based on wafer-level Cu/SiO₂ HB technology, com-
panies such as Teledyne in the United States achieved het-
erogeneous integration of 250 nm InP heterojunction bipolar 
transistor (HBT) and 130 nm Si CMOS [83], demonstrat-
ing high-performance millimeter-wave ICs. The InP HBT 
wafer was processed with an Au/benzocyclobutene (BCB) 
interconnect layer and prepared with a SiO₂ surface embed-
ded with micro-copper blocks. The Si CMOS wafer featured 
six layers of copper interconnect wiring. After bonding, the 
CMOS wafer substrate was thinned to 10 μm and electri-
cally connected through Al pads. Tests showed that the HB 
process did not affect the performance of the transistors.

Current HB technology demonstrates significant advan-
tages in improving chip integration density, enhancing data 
transmission bandwidth, and optimizing thermal manage-
ment, particularly showing promising application prospects 
in fields such as HBM, 3D chip integration, and CMOS 
image sensors. However, several technical bottlenecks 
remain, including interface reliability, process complex-
ity, material compatibility, and cost control for large-scale 
production. Although progress has been made through the 
introduction of novel auxiliary materials and optimization 
of process parameters (such as surface treatment and tem-
perature control), further breakthroughs are still needed to 
achieve higher precision and lower-cost mass production.
7  Conclusions
This review centers on the core theme of “Cu–Cu HB tech-
nology,” systematically constructing a comprehensive frame-
work spanning from physical mechanisms to macroscopic 
applications. It aims to provide a thorough overview of the 
research progress, critical technological breakthroughs, and 
future potential of this technology. Adopting a progressive 
logic of “theory–process–application–trend,” the review 
establishes a panoramic perspective from microscopic mech-
anisms to macroscopic industrial applications, emphasizing 
both technical depth and practical implementation. This 
approach offers a clear roadmap for subsequent research, 
presenting a holistic view of Cu–Cu HB technology, deliv-
ering a systematic summary of its current advancements 
while charting directions for future breakthroughs. As an 
advanced interconnect technology, Cu–Cu HB demonstrates 
significant advantages in HPC, high-speed communication, 
and advanced packaging. By enabling direct copper-to-
copper bonding, it achieves sub-micron or even nanoscale 
interconnect pitches, substantially enhancing chip integra-
tion density and data transmission bandwidth while reducing 
signal latency and energy loss. Furthermore, HB drives the 
development of 2.5D and 3D packaging technologies, facili-
tating vertical chip stacking to improve system performance 
and energy efficiency. As shown in Fig. 13, in future Cu–Cu 
mixed bonding will develop toward low-temperature bond-
ing and high integration, while being extensively applied 
in heterogeneous integration and high-bandwidth storage 
and AI chips to meet the demands for high density and low 
latency. In summary, Cu–Cu HB technology is poised to 
continue driving electronic devices toward higher perfor-
mance, integration density, and reliability, solidifying its 
role as a cornerstone of next-generation microelectronics.
