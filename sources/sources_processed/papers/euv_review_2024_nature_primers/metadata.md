## Abstract
Extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography uses 13.5 nm wavelength light to pattern semiconductor devices at the most advanced technology nodes. This primer covers the fundamentals and current state of EUV lithography, including source technology, optics, mask infrastructure, resist materials, and computational lithography. EUV was applied to high-volume manufacturing in 2019, with ASML's TWINSCAN NXE systems operating at 0.33 NA. High-NA EUV tools (0.55 NA) became available for early evaluation by chipmakers in 2024, promising resolution down to 8 nm half-pitch. Key challenges include stochastic effects (photon shot noise and chemical stochastic effects in resists), defectivity management, mask pellicle durability, and the need for higher source power. The primer discusses how improvements in resist materials, computational lithography, and process control are needed to enable high-NA EUV for production.

## Key Points
- Authoritative primer on EUV lithography fundamentals and current HVM status
- Covers the transition from 0.33 NA to 0.55 NA (High-NA) EUV tools
- Discusses stochastic effects as a fundamental challenge: photon shot noise, acid diffusion, resist chemistry
- Reviews EUV source technology, reflective optics, and mask infrastructure
- Addresses resist materials: chemically amplified resists (CAR) vs. metal oxide resists (MOR)
