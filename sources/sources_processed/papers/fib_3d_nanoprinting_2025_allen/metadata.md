## Abstract
The focused ion beam (FIB) microscope serves as a high-resolution machining instrument capable of site-selectively removing material down to the nanoscale. Beyond material removal, focused ion beam induced deposition (FIBID) enables direct writing of intricate nanostructures. This research investigates innovative approaches to three-dimensional nanoprinting leveraging helium and neon FIBs to construct nanoscale heterostructures, including multimaterial designs and deposits featuring engineered internal voids. The superior spatial resolution of helium and neon FIBs is leveraged to fabricate precise multimaterial architectures, using isotope effects to create satellite deposits and dose-controlled implantation of gaseous ions to engineer internal voids. Advanced electron microscopy techniques provide detailed chemical and structural analysis, exposing buried interfaces and compositional transformations. The findings support FIBID's evolution into a versatile platform for functional nanomaterials design, establishing possibilities for emerging nanoscale technologies and devices.

## Key Points
- Demonstrates FIBID 3D nanoprinting using helium and neon FIBs (not traditional Ga+)
- Creates multimaterial nanoscale heterostructures with engineered internal voids
- Exploits isotope effects and dose-controlled ion implantation for novel architectures
- Advanced TEM characterization reveals buried interfaces and compositional transformations
- Positions FIBID as a functional nanomaterials design platform beyond traditional FIB milling

### 1. Introduction

Focused ion beams (FIBs) can both remove and add material with nanometric precision. Traditionally, their role as a "scalpel" has dominated, with high-resolution milling making FIBs indispensable for cross-sectioning, trimming, and shaping specimens. In contrast, the FIB's role as a "printer" via focused ion beam induced deposition (FIBID) has remained comparatively underexplored.

The principle of FIBID rests on the localized decomposition of adsorbed precursor molecules introduced via a gas injector needle. A solid deposit forms from the non-volatile reaction products. While secondary electrons are generally considered the primary drivers of dissociation, in FIBID with heavier ions, direct momentum transfer and energy transfer from surface atoms also contribute.

The resolution revolution brought by the helium ion microscope (HIM), with its sub-nanometer probe and minimal lateral scatter, means FIBID resolution can now rival FEBID. He-FIBID has produced nanowires with diameters of 10 nm and freestanding nanopillars 32 nm wide yet several microns in length.

Benefits of He-FIBID over Ga-FIBID: (1) gaseous ion species avoids metallic ion contamination of insulating deposits; (2) lower sputtering power enables deposition onto delicate ultra-thin substrates; (3) hollow core nanopillars can be fabricated by concurrent milling during deposition.

This article explores: (1) fabrication of nanoscale heterostructures from metallic (W(CO)6) and dielectric (PMCPS) precursors; (2) isotopic effects in Ne-FIBID producing satellite deposits; (3) ion-induced swelling for internal void engineering.

### 2. Results and Discussion

#### 2.1 Nanostructure arrays and multimaterial heterostructures

Height-modulated nanopillar arrays (~35 nm wide, 300-900 nm height) demonstrated via He-FIBID. Nanoring arrays with ~75 nm inner diameter and ~90 nm height. Multimaterial heterostructures include:
- Vertically-stacked trilayer nanocapacitor (metallic-insulating-metallic)
- Metallic nanopillar with insulator cap (for nanocapacitive probing)
- Nanopillar-in-nanosleeve structures (for insulated nanoelectrodes)
- Reverse configuration: dielectric nanopillar encased by metallic nanosleeve

Since the ion species (He) is non-metallic, insulating layers are not contaminated with metal ions.

#### 2.2 Composition and structure analysis

Upon increasing FIB species mass, deposit purity (tungsten content) is enhanced due to more efficient momentum transfer to cleave chemical bonds. 4D-STEM grain mapping revealed polycrystalline metallic deposits with average grain size ~13 nm, while PMCPS deposits were amorphous (~30% Si, 25% C, 40% O).

#### 2.3 Satellite deposition

Ne-FIBID produces satellite deposits due to isotopic mass separation: ~90% 20Ne and ~9% 22Ne follow different beam paths. The satellite deposit height ratio (~1:10) quantitatively measures relative isotope abundance.

#### 2.4 Nanostructures with internal voids

Hollow core nanopillars (~28 nm wide with ~2 nm core) formed by localized He-FIB milling during growth. Larger voids created by Ne ion implantation at higher dose (0.5 pA beam current producing porous nanopillars). He-FIBID with PMCPS precursor at high dose produces single large void from helium nanobubble coalescence.

SRIM simulations: Ne ions concentrate at ~20 nm depth, He ions at ~300 nm depth, explaining different void distribution patterns.

### 3. Conclusion

FIBID using focused He and Ne ion beams enables versatile fabrication of heterostructures in the 100 nm size range and below. Sequential deposition of conductive and insulating layers without contaminating dielectric components. Internal void engineering provides additional structural control. Isotope separation into individual nanostructures demonstrated.

Applications range from nanoelectrodes for transmembrane biosensing to nanoscale reaction vessels for confined chemical reactions.

### 4. Experimental Section

Performed using Zeiss ORION NanoFab microscope (He, Ne from GFIS; Ga from LMIS). Precursors: W(CO)6 (metallic) and PMCPS (dielectric) via OmniGIS II. He beam: 0.5 nm probe, 25 keV, 1-3 pA. Ne beam: 2 nm probe, 25 keV, 0.5-2 pA. TEM/STEM analysis at 80/300 kV using FEI TitanX. 4D-STEM with non-negative matrix factorization for grain classification.
