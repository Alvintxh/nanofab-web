## Abstract
Atomic layer deposition (ALD) is a surface-controlled chemical vapour deposition method in which materials are prepared one atomic layer at a time. Film thickness can be controlled very precisely, and ALD allows uniform and conformal coverage of large areas with complex three-dimensional structures. This primer describes ALD's precise film thickness control and covers its implementation in high-volume manufacturing in the semiconductor, solar, and display industry, along with other emerging application fields. The article integrates both historical development and recent innovations including atomic-scale area-selective ALD and high-throughput spatial ALD (SALD) exceeding 4,000 wafers/hour, while highlighting emerging precursor and in-situ characterization strategies and addressing commercialization challenges such as slow deposition rates and limited precursor availability.

## Key Points
- Comprehensive primer on ALD fundamentals, covering self-limiting surface reactions, precursor chemistry, and growth mechanisms
- Covers high-volume manufacturing applications in semiconductor, solar, and display industries
- Discusses area-selective ALD for bottom-up patterning and spatial ALD for high throughput
- Addresses key challenges: deposition speed, precursor availability, and scaling to new materials
- Reviews in-situ characterization techniques for ALD process monitoring and optimization
