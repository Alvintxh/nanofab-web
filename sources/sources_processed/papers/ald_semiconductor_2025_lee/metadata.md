## Abstract
This comprehensive review examines technological advancements in semiconductor manufacturing, focusing on three core techniques: atomic layer deposition (ALD), atomic layer etching (ALE), and neutral beam etching (NBE). These methods are recognized for their precision at the atomic scale and prove essential for fabricating next-generation silicon photonics and optoelectronic devices, as well as RF/power compound semiconductors. ALD provides superior control over thin film growth with uniformity and material conformity. ALE enables precise layer-by-layer material removal, making it ideal for high-aspect-ratio structures. NBE minimizes surface damage, which is particularly critical for GaN-based semiconductors and device reliability. The authors assess how these integrated technologies enhance semiconductor device performance while overcoming traditional method limitations. Their combined application drives innovations in micro-LEDs, optical communication, and high-frequency power electronic devices, making these processes indispensable for next-generation semiconductor advancement.

## Key Points
- Reviews three atomic-scale semiconductor manufacturing techniques (ALD, ALE, NBE) in a unified framework
- ALD provides conformal, uniform thin films critical for advanced node devices
- ALE enables atomic-precision material removal for high-aspect-ratio structures
- NBE minimizes surface damage for III-V compound semiconductor devices
- Applications span micro-LEDs, optical communication, and RF/power electronics

## 1. Introduction

With rapid semiconductor industry growth, Moore's law remains a guiding principle for continuous device advancement. The prediction that transistor numbers double every two years drives ongoing size reduction, requiring "ultra-precision and ultra-fine technologies" for device performance and reliability.

Etching and deposition form the foundation for high-performance semiconductor devices, enabling miniaturization and increased functional density. Figure 1 illustrates semiconductor manufacturing trends from 2000 to 2035, showing progression from geometric scaling at 90 nm (introducing strained Si and copper interconnections) through equivalent scaling eras with high-k gate dielectrics, to heterogeneous post-Moore scaling incorporating FinFETs and gate-all-around (GAA) transistors.

GAA transistors provide superior channel control through three-dimensional structure, with the gate surrounding channels on multiple sides, enhancing gate control and reducing short-channel effects. Advanced technologies like compound field-effect transistors (CFETs), 2D semiconductors, and hybrid integration promise continued Moore's law sustainability while expanding into more-than-Moore functionalities including photonic integration, quantum technologies, and neuromorphic computing.

These advancements heavily rely on nanoscale etching and deposition processes—atomic layer deposition (ALD), atomic layer etching (ALE), and neutral beam etching (NBE)—which are critical for achieving required precision and performance in next-generation devices.

## 2. Etching Techniques

### 2.1. Definition and Background

The etching process removes material from surfaces through chemical or physical methods, playing key roles in semiconductor manufacturing. Precise control of etch depth, profile, surface roughness, and uniformity is critical for ensuring micro- and nanoelectronic device performance and reliability.

Wet etching utilizes chemical reactions in bath environments, known for low cost, ease of implementation, and high material selectivity. Dry etching, performed in vacuum chambers through physical and chemical reactions, provides greater precision depth control, profile selectivity, and ability to define critical feature dimensions.

Table 1 compares wet and dry etching techniques. Wet etching offers low cost and easy implementation with high selectivity for most materials, but cannot adequately define features smaller than 1 μm, causes wafer contamination, and maintains inconsistent etch rates. Dry etching enables deep etching ranges with higher rates and easy automation for defining small features (<100 nm), but demonstrates poor selectivity, low throughput, uses toxic gases, and poses potential radiation damage.

Wet etching typically produces isotropic results (except in crystalline materials), while dry etching is typically anisotropic.

Table 2 highlights major etching parameters essential for optimizing semiconductor manufacturing. Etching rate—the material removal speed—increases efficiency but can produce rough surfaces and structural damage. Etching rate nonuniformity, influenced by plasma power, gas flow, temperature, and pressure, affects consistency across wafers. Selectivity, the ratio of etching rates between different materials, ensures precise targeted material removal. Critical dimensions involve shaped and smooth etched feature characteristics influenced by etching and passivation cycle balance. Aspect ratio dependent etching (ARDE) involves variation in etch rates for structures with different aspect ratios.

In Si-based CMOS, GaN-based, and SiC-based devices, dry etching is crucial for creating high-precision structures. Si-based CMOS uses dry etching for achieving high aspect ratios and fine line patterns. GaN and SiC devices depend on these techniques for producing precise features and smooth surfaces. Wet etching is primarily used for surface treatment and defect characterization, offering high selectivity and low damage.

### 2.2. Overview of Etching Techniques

Etching techniques classify into wet etching and dry etching. Wet etching uses liquid chemicals to remove materials and includes several sub-techniques. Chemical etching, the traditional form, uses chemical solutions to dissolve specific materials. Defect-selective etching selectively etches materials based on defect densities, allowing targeted removal of defective areas. Aqueous etchants, commonly used in chemical etching for dissolving specific materials in water-based solutions, are easy to handle and environmentally friendly.

Electrochemical etching uses electrical current to drive etching processes in electrolyte solutions, allowing more precise control. Electroless etching does not require external power sources, relying on self-sustaining chemical reactions once initiated. Anodic etching uses positive voltage to drive processes and creates porous structures in materials like silicon. Photoelectrochemical (PEC) etching uses light to enhance electrochemical etching, offering precise etching in compound semiconductors.

Dry etching uses gases or plasma to remove materials and offers high precision for very small feature sizes. Physical etching methods like electron beam etching and ion beam etching use focused beams for nanoscale etching. Electron beam etching offers high precision for advanced nanofabrication, while ion beam etching effectively achieves high aspect ratio structures. Laser etching also uses laser during processes.

Reactive ion etching (RIE) combines physical sputtering with chemical reactions for precise etching, widely used in microelectronics for creating high-aspect-ratio features. Plasma etching uses plasma to generate reactive species that etch materials, effective for pattern transfer and surface modification.

Advanced techniques include ALE, which alternates between adsorption and reaction steps to remove material one atomic layer at a time. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) etching uses plasma generated by inductive coupling, providing high etch rates and good control. Neutral beam etching (NBE) uses neutral beams to avoid charge build-up and photon-radiation damage, making it ideal for ultra-high-density integrated circuits.

### 2.3. Advanced Dry Etching Techniques

ALE and NBE are essential for wide-ranging advanced semiconductor applications, including high-density integrated circuits, 3D NAND flash memory, advanced logic devices like FinFETs and GAA FETs, high-frequency and power devices like GaN HEMTs and SiC MOSFETs, and optoelectronic devices like VCSELs and photodetectors.

Both ALE and NBE provide excellent etching process control, enabling atomic-level precision. For fabricating nanoscale feature devices, these techniques are becoming increasingly common in advanced semiconductor applications. ALE removes material layer by layer through self-limiting reactions, ensuring each cycle removes only a single atomic layer. This minimizes over-etching risks and reduces surface roughness, essential for maintaining fine feature integrity.

NBE uses neutralized particles to avoid charging and radiation damage typically associated with ion beams, making it suitable for etching delicate structures without compromising functionality.

ALE and NBE capabilities particularly benefit advanced semiconductor device production like FinFETs, 3D NAND flash memory, and high-performance integrated circuits. With CMOS technology development, reliability issues become more challenging, especially for 5 nm nodes and beyond. As critical dimensions shrink, device electrical characteristics become increasingly sensitive to feature size variations. Even slight etch depth or profile deviations lead to significant performance changes, including threshold voltage shifts, increased leakage currents, and reduced drive currents.

GAA transistor structures and advanced etching techniques achieve precise lateral and vertical dimension control. Figure 3 illustrates lateral and vertical GAA structures. In etching 20 nm linewidth SiGe/Si structures, parameters like pressure and power significantly influence etch rate, selectivity, and morphology. Higher pressure and power improve etch rate and profile definition, but excessive values create depth and uniformity inconsistencies. Therefore, precise parameter control is essential for accuracy in advanced semiconductor devices like GAA transistors.

These devices require extremely fine feature sizes and precise etching control, effectively achieved using ALE and NBE. As semiconductor technology advances, new materials and increasingly complex structures introduce requirements that ALE and NBE adapt to, providing flexibility needed for etching diverse materials with different properties.

#### 2.3.1. Atomic Layer Etching (ALE)

ALE is a highly precise technique critical for fabricating nanoscale semiconductor devices. Alternating between adsorption and reaction steps, ALE achieves single atomic layer removal per cycle, providing exceptional control and minimizing surface roughness. This method, derived from ALD techniques, involves sequential exposure to different reactive gases with intermediate purging steps ensuring precise layer-by-layer removal and atomic-scale accuracy.

ALE is particularly advantageous in advanced 3D integrated circuit fabrication and memory devices. In 3D IC manufacturing, ALE addresses creating complex 3D transistor architectures like GAA and multi-bridge-channel FETs (MBCFETs). By enabling atomic-scale etching, ALE provides exceptional morphology and depth control, ensuring precise nanoscale feature patterning.

ALE plays a pivotal role in advanced memory applications like MRAM and other non-volatile memory technologies. It enables accurate etching of metal nitrides (TiN and AlN) and high-k dielectric materials (HfO2), resulting in reduced leakage currents, improved surface smoothness, and enhanced depth control. This unique etching approach offers extremely high selectivity and control, ideal for fabricating intricate nanoscale structures while minimizing substrate damage and achieving smooth, uniform etching profiles.

##### 2.3.1.1. Principles of ALE

ALE functions through two principal mechanisms: thermal ALE and plasma ALE. Thermal ALE utilizes selective surface layer removal through chemical reactions forming volatile compounds. This process is self-limiting, allowing precise etching control and automatically stopping once the modified layer is fully removed. Thermal ALE advantages include isotropic etching capability with minimal surface damage.

Plasma ALE provides precise etching control and is effective for materials requiring high temperatures or complex precursors. This is attributed to plasma dissociation capabilities enabling desired results.

A typical ALE cycle comprises four main steps. Initially, a reactive gas like chlorine adsorbs onto the etching material surface. A purging step follows, removing excess reactive gas and preventing unwanted reactions. Subsequently, ion source gas like argon is introduced and plasma is activated, ionizing the gas and providing energy necessary to remove the adsorbed material layer. A final purge clears reaction byproducts. This cyclic process ensures each cycle removes only a single atomic layer, providing atomic-scale precision and minimizing substrate damage.

Figure 4(b) illustrates the relationship between removal energy and etched amount per cycle (EPC). The ALE process window is characterized by two regions: the energy barrier limited region, where removal energy is too low, resulting in incomplete etching and low EPC, and the sputtering limited region, where removal energy is too high, leading to over-etching and substrate damage. Maintaining removal energy within the ALE window is crucial for achieving self-limiting and controlled etching.

Figure 4(c) provides a schematic ALE process representation. During each cycle, a reactive gas adsorbs onto the surface forming a reactive layer, which is then removed using ionized argon (Ar+), allowing repeated precise layer-by-layer etching. The inserted illustration highlights ALE's self-limiting nature, where the etching rate reaches saturation, ensuring precise removal process control.

Demonstrating ALE advantages in achieving precise, controlled, and uniform etching, Figure 5 illustrates three fundamental aspects: synergy, self-limited etching, and cyclic etch. Synergy is demonstrated for silicon nitride etching through hydrogen and fluorinated plasma step combinations. Initial hydrogen plasma exposure modifies silicon nitride surfaces, making them more reactive to subsequent fluorinated plasma. This synergistic effect enhances etch rate and selectivity. Increased etch depth when both plasmas are used sequentially, compared to individual plasma use, confirms enhanced efficiency due to synergy.

Self-limited etching is a critical ALE feature, ensuring each cycle removes only a single atomic layer. Figure 5(a) shows average etch yield in ALE at different F/Ar+ ratios, comparing Si3N4 and SiO2 etching behaviors with significant cyclic etching correlation. As the F/Ar+ ratio increases, chemical etching efficiency improves, but exceeding a critical point limits etch by ion bombardment. High F/Ar+ ratios cause fluorocarbon layer accumulation on surfaces, decreasing etch yield like insufficient residue removal between cycles in cyclic etching.

Figure 5(b) illustrates the complete thermal ALE cycle, showing two self-limiting half-reaction processes. In half-reaction A, step I is the adsorption phase, where the precursor self-limits by adsorbing onto material surfaces. Once surfaces are fully covered, reaction automatically stops. In half-reaction B, step III is the material removal phase, where the second reactant converts the adsorbed layer into volatile products, removing one atomic layer in self-limiting manner. This self-limiting characteristic ensures only one atomic layer is removed per cycle, providing extremely high etching precision. These features ensure high precision, control, and uniformity, essential for producing advanced semiconductor devices.

##### 2.3.1.2. Applications of ALE

Evaluating GaN material performance using ALE with Cl2 and either He or Ar plasma, research demonstrated that while Ar-based ALE processes show self-limiting behavior and lower GaN damage, He-based ALE processes lead to deeper ion implantation and higher electrical degradation. This research highlights appropriate etching plasma source selection importance for damage minimization and performance optimization.

Regarding device characteristic optimization using ALE, researchers studied ALE technology and InAlN/GaN heterostructure surface treatment. After appropriate ALE etching cycles, two-dimensional electron gas (2-DEG) density and surface roughness improved by 15% and 11.4%, respectively, compared to digital etching, and sheet resistance (Rsh) decreased by 6.7%.

Figure 6(a) illustrates high etch selectivity achieved through thermal ALE at 250 °C, demonstrating negligible removal of SiN, SiO2, and TiN after 1000 ALE cycles. This high selectivity stems from targeted process chemistry and absence of energetic ions and radicals, allowing precise specific material removal without affecting other device components.

Figure 6(b) demonstrates significant plasma-assisted ALE benefits, including exceptional etch depth uniformity across wafers and flat silicon etch fronts, validating capability to produce atomically smooth surfaces.

ALE can provide significant advantages in enhancing normally-off GaN recessed gate MIS-HEMT performance, including breakdown voltage, low leakage current, and dynamic on-resistance. ALE application for low-damage AlGaN surface etching has also shown optimization for high-frequency GaN HEMTs. The precision and control capabilities enable high-quality interface creation with low surface damage and well-defined features, critical for high-power applications.

Figure 7(a) shows the precise AlGaN layer etching from 25 nm to 3.7 nm in cross-sectional images. Figure 7(b) presents surface roughness measurement showing root mean square roughness (Ra) of 0.4 nm over a 1 μm² area, indicating smooth ALE surfaces. This smoothness is crucial for minimizing carrier scattering and surface traps while enhancing GaN-based device electron mobility.

Table 3 shows MIS-HEMT performance with ALE-fabricated recessed gate devices, demonstrating a competitive balance of high current density (608 mA mm−1), controllable threshold voltage (+2.0 V), and high breakdown voltage (720 V at 1 μA mm−1), suitable for high power applications. Comparison devices fabricated using ICP etching achieve higher maximum current density of 836 mA mm−1 in double-channel designs but exhibit lower breakdown voltage (705 V at 1 μA mm−1) and less precise threshold voltage control (+0.5 V). The dynamic on-resistance (Ron,sp) for ALE-fabricated devices is 1.27 mΩ cm², superior to the 1.48 mΩ cm² observed in ICP-etched devices.

Additionally, ALE-fabricated devices demonstrate significantly reduced surface damage, contributing to enhanced reliability. The etched surface smoothness achieved by ALE, characterized by RMS roughness of 0.4 nm, markedly improves compared to 1.2 nm obtained using ICP etching. These quantitative improvements in breakdown voltage (+15 V), threshold voltage stability (+1.5 V), and reduced surface roughness (66.67% improvement) underline ALE superiority over ICP etching, particularly for applications requiring precise electrical control and high reliability.

Research illustrates ALE advantages in achieving low-damage, self-limiting AlGaN etching processes using O₂ and BCl₃ plasma. This process ensures high etching precision, minimal surface damage, and excellent etch depth control, leading to improved Schottky diode electrical characteristics compared to traditional digital etching methods. The ideality factor was found more uniform and lower for diodes fabricated using ALE (approximately 1.0 at 0.3 V forward voltage) compared to digital etching (approximately 2.0 at the same voltage). This significant 50% reduction in ideality factor indicates ALE causes less plasma-induced damage, resulting in better electrical characteristics including lower reverse current and higher zero-bias barrier heights.

Further assessing etching damage impacts, photoluminescence (PL) intensity was measured at approximately 5 nm etch depths. Samples etched using ALE showed approximately 30% higher near-band-edge PL intensity compared to digital etching samples. This suggests ALE produces fewer non-radiative recombination centers like nitrogen vacancies, which degrade device performance.

#### 2.3.2. Neutral-Beam Etching (NBE)

NBE represents significant advancement in GaN-based HEMT and light-emitting diode (LED) etching processes. This method effectively addresses the critical plasma-induced damage challenge prevalent in conventional etching techniques like ICP-RIE. GaN materials, highly valued for high-power and high-frequency applications, face challenges in achieving normally-off operation in GaN-based HEMTs due to plasma-induced damage from techniques like gate recessing. NBE offers potential solutions for minimizing such damage and enhancing device performance.

NBE is specifically designed to minimize plasma-induced damage on semiconductor materials, particularly GaN-based devices. Conventional plasma etching methods cause significant surface damage through ion bombardment, resulting in increased leakage currents, reduced breakdown voltages, and degraded device performance. NBE addresses these issues by providing a damage-free etching environment through ion neutralization and UV photon reduction, minimizing surface damage and improving electrical characteristics.

Beyond GaN-based device success, NBE demonstrates significant potential in fabricating 3D Fin-FET structures, cornerstones of modern 3D IC technologies. Critical advantages include selective germanium etching capability, a material increasingly used in advanced Fin-FET designs for superior electron mobility. The neutralization process in NBE minimizes ion bombardment and plasma-induced damage, ensuring precise material removal without affecting adjacent layers. This high selectivity preserves germanium fin structural integrity while maintaining clean interfaces, crucial for achieving high carrier mobility and reliable device performance.

NBE's atomic-scale etching capabilities and damage-free nature give it significant potential for fabricating high-aspect-ratio structures required in Fin-FETs. These characteristics suggest NBE could become an important 3D IC manufacturing tool.

##### 2.3.2.1. Principles of NBE

NBE operates using neutralized ion beams that effectively etch material surfaces without typical conventional plasma etching undesirable effects. The NBE system consists of a dual-chamber setup: a plasma generation chamber and an etching chamber, separated by a carbon electrode with high aspect ratio apertures. This configuration allows plasma ions to be neutralized as they pass through carbon apertures, significantly reducing charged particles and UV photons.

The NBE system ensures etched surfaces maintain integrity and performance, making it crucial for advanced semiconductor device development.

The carbon apertures in NBE systems play critical roles in its effectiveness. The carbon aperture array is crucial for achieving high-efficiency ion neutralization and substrate protection during etching. Graphite is commonly selected as aperture material due to its excellent thermal and electrical properties, ensuring durability under intense plasma exposure. These apertures' high aspect ratio is essential for the neutralization process. As plasma ions generated in the plasma chamber pass through narrow, elongated carbon apertures, they are neutralized, transforming from charged particles into neutral atoms.

This transformation occurs because neutral atoms, unlike charged ions, do not cause significant etching material damage. The high aspect ratio design also enhances neutralization by providing extended paths for ions to interact with carbon walls, converting charged ions into neutral particles. Moreover, it minimizes energy loss by maintaining neutralized particle directionality and kinetic energy while reducing ion-wall collisions.

Additionally, the high aspect ratio design effectively filters out UV photons, which are another conventional plasma etching damage source. The carbon aperture array is further engineered to support pressure differentials between plasma source and processing regions. This configuration reduces background gas collisions, preserving neutral beam energy and ensuring precise etching performance. By minimizing ion bombardment and UV photon exposure, carbon aperture design in NBE significantly reduces surface damage, leading to improved etched device electrical characteristics. This makes NBE particularly advantageous for applications requiring high precision and minimal damage.

##### 2.3.2.2. Applications of NBE

NBE is widely applied in high-density semiconductor device fabrication, including advanced logic and memory devices. Its ability to produce high-resolution patterns with minimal surface damage also suits GaN devices for millimeter-wave applications. Additionally, precise etch depth control and excellent surface roughness for recessed gate E-mode AlGaN/GaN HEMTs exhibit excellent electrical characteristics. Beyond technological advantages, NBE introduces distinct cost implications. While initial equipment investment is higher due to neutral beam generation system requirements, it reduces long-term costs by minimizing surface damage and improving process yields. Traditional plasma etching, with lower upfront costs, often incurs higher operational expenses due to greater defect mitigation needs. These dynamics make NBE ideal for high-precision applications like GaN-based HEMTs and micro-LEDs, where reliability and yield outweigh initial costs.

Furthermore, when applied to n-GaN surfaces treated with fluorine ions and neutral beams, NBE optimizes ohmic contacts and exhibits lower contact resistivity. This is because the fluorine neutral beam removes nitrogen from surfaces. Compared to fluorine ion beams, although both reduce contact resistivity, the fluorine neutral beam is more effective, likely due to lack of charge-induced defect formation.

The etching gas choice in NBE is also crucial. Research studying HBr and Cl₂ neutral beam etching effects found that HBr achieves thinner reaction layers than Cl₂ because the Br atom diameter is 1.2 times larger than Cl atoms. Furthermore, HBr generates less volatile etching products due to higher byproduct boiling points.

Research demonstrated that adjusting carbon aperture aspect ratios in NBE significantly affects surface roughness and plasma-induced damage. With a carbon aperture aspect ratio of 10, the RMS roughness was significantly lower at 0.452 nm compared to 1.024 nm for aspect ratio 2, highlighting smoother etching achieved with better neutralization. This adjustment in aperture design effectively reduced surface damage, leading to improved AlGaN/GaN HEMT electrical performance.

NBE also provides solutions by utilizing neutral particle beams that eliminate charged particles and UV photons responsible for plasma damage. This process ensures precise etching depth control and significantly reduces etched surface damage. Research demonstrated that NBE-etched GaN HEMTs reduced isolation leakage current by tenfold (1 μA mm−1 to 0.1 μA mm−1 at 4 V) compared to plasma beam (PB) etching, with RMS surface roughness improving from 2.226 nm to 0.427 nm. NBE also increased soft breakdown voltage (SBV) to over 210 V, compared to 25.6 V for PB, and hard breakdown voltage to over 800 V (PB: 600–700 V).

Furthermore, using PL data, research demonstrated 30% higher near-band-edge PL intensity in NBE-etched GaN, indicating fewer defects and enhanced optical efficiency without forward voltage increases.

Research demonstrated superior interface quality and electrical performance of high-frequency GaN HEMTs when utilizing NBE compared to ICP-RIE. High-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images show smoother and more uniform gate recess interfaces with NBE. The electrical performance, including noise and frequency response, highlights that NBE-processed devices exhibit better direct current (DC) and high-frequency characteristics. Compared to ICP-RIE results, on-resistance improves by 52%, transconductance increases by 22%, and the noise figure reduces by 31% using NBE. Additionally, cutoff frequency increases by 28%, and drain current density enhances. This improvement is attributed to minimal NBE etching damage, making it an excellent high-performance GaN device fabrication technique.

Research investigated NBE and ICP-RIE performance in GaN-based micro-LED fabrication. Results demonstrated that sidewall defects caused by plasma etching are significantly reduced by NBE, thereby enhancing LED efficiency. NBE-etched devices exhibit nearly consistent external quantum efficiency (EQE) across different array sizes, with EQEmax being 6.95% for 4 × 4 arrays (40 μm) and 7.11% for 2 × 2 arrays (80 μm). In contrast, ICP-RIE-etched devices show significant EQE decreases as size decreases, with EQEmax values of 8.13% for 2 × 2 arrays (80 μm) and 7.48% for 4 × 4 arrays (40 μm). This demonstrates that NBE maintains more consistent performance, particularly in smaller-sized micro-LEDs, reducing the size effect and enhancing overall device efficiency.

Through cathodoluminescence (CL) analysis, samples etched with NBE have stronger and more sustained radiative recombination signals at sidewalls, while ICP-RIE etched sample signals are weaker and decay more quickly. This indicates that UV light generated during ICP-RIE processes damages mesa sidewalls, creating more defects and reducing radiative recombination behavior. These findings highlight NBE technology's significant advantages in fabricating high-performance GaN-based micro-LEDs.

## 3. Deposition Techniques

Thin film technology is an advanced approach aimed at improving structural, electrical, magnetic, optical, and mechanical properties of bulk materials. It has found widespread application in semiconductor devices, integrated circuits, transistors, liquid crystal displays, light-emitting diodes, solar cells, sensors, and micro-electromechanical systems (MEMSs). Distinctive thin film material properties are crucial for technological advancement of various electronic, electrical, magnetic, and optical devices. These films are created using various physical or chemical methods, each essential for producing ultra-thin materials known for uniform, conformal, and controllable thickness. As atomic and near-atomic scale manufacturing (ACSM) evolves, the necessity of depositing high-quality, impurity-free thin films for laminated structures becomes crucial. This is especially true when film thickness approaches atomic dimensions, posing significant challenges for conventional deposition methods.

Traditional thin film deposition processes often face challenges in achieving balance between conformality, precise thickness control, and desired functionality material selection. Figure 14 shows various thin film deposition techniques. Established methods such as physical vapor deposition (PVD) and chemical vapor deposition (CVD), sputtering, spin coating, dip coating, and spray pyrolysis have been extensively utilized. However, achieving conformal film deposition over complex three-dimensional structures with high aspect ratios challenges techniques like PVD, and electrodeposition is limited to conductive materials only. Although CVD is adaptable and produces high-quality films, it has drawbacks including high processing temperatures and difficulty obtaining consistent thickness on complex geometries.

Recently, an advanced vapor-phase deposition process known as ALD has addressed these limitations by providing precise film thickness control and conformality due to its self-limiting surface reactions. This novel technique has been extensively investigated, effectively satisfying advanced micro/nano semiconductor manufacturing urgent demands. The fundamental advantages of this technology were compared to other deposition methods like electroplating, spin coating, sputtering, PVD, and CVD. It has potential to overcome current challenges and establish new atomic level thin film technology standards.

### 3.1. Traditional Deposition Technology

Thin film deposition techniques are systematically classified into four distinct categories based on deposition process nature, as illustrated in Figure 14. This classification fundamentally relies on whether deposition mechanisms in PVD or CVD are primarily physical or chemical. Additionally, solution deposition thin film methods include various techniques including chemical bath deposition (CBD), spray pyrolysis, sol–gel processing, spin coating, and dip-coating. Each method utilizes chemical reactions in solutions to form films. Key parameters that must be carefully controlled in chemical reactions include solution concentration, temperature, pH levels, and reaction time. Precise control of these factors is important for achieving desired film properties and ensuring film suitability for specific applications.

In contrast, physical processes such as PVD, sputtering, molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), electron beam evaporation, and thermal evaporation primarily rely on material physical transfer from a source to substrate under vacuum conditions. The chemical category includes methods such as CVD, plasma-enhanced CVD (PECVD), metal–organic CVD (MOCVD), and ALD, which involve chemical reactions at substrate surfaces to grow films. Finally, advanced CVD technique classifications include methods such as mist-CVD and hydride vapor phase epitaxy (HVPE).

Mist-CVD operates without vacuum environments, utilizing aerosolized precursors for efficient deposition, ideal for large-area applications. This technique has been effectively employed to grow high-quality Ga₂O₃ films, demonstrating low cost, simplicity, and various substrate compatibility advantages. For example, high-quality crystalline NiO/β-Ga₂O₃ p–n heterojunctions grown via mist-CVD exhibit excellent crystallinity, showcasing potential for advanced semiconductor device applications.

HVPE is primarily employed for high-quality single-crystal growth, particularly in III–V semiconductors. Additionally, HVPE has been effectively utilized in depositing aluminum nitride (AlN), a material renowned for its wide bandgap and high thermal conductivity. HVPE-grown AlN films exhibit excellent structural properties, including high crystallinity and smooth surface morphology, critical for applications requiring high breakdown voltages and large-area substrates. Furthermore, HVPE facilitates thick AlN layer growth at high growth rates, enabling free-standing AlN wafer production serving as substrates for III-nitride device structures. These advancements underscore HVPE versatility in supporting advanced semiconductor applications like deep ultraviolet optoelectronics and acoustic devices. These advanced methods expand CVD technique scope by offering unique advantages like cost-effectiveness, diverse substrate compatibility, and high crystal quality, catering to next-generation electronic and optoelectronic device demands.

Each thin film deposition technology has unique advantages and inherent limitations, which greatly affect their application in various technical fields. PVD processes like sputtering and evaporation offer excellent film purity and composition control due to their vacuum-based deposition environments, facilitating high-quality film production with precise microstructural characteristics. Nevertheless, there are process challenges including material selection limitations, complex geometry conformal coverage difficulties, and potential film quality issues.

Conversely, CVD processes are often preferred for their ability to produce uniform large and intricately shaped area coatings, a capability PVD cannot consistently achieve. CVD's wide material range handling capability, combined with its effectiveness in filling high aspect ratio structures, renders it essential for semiconductor device and integrated circuit fabrication. However, CVD methods typically require higher substrate temperatures and may introduce unnecessary impurities from precursor chemicals, creating thermal budget and material compatibility challenges.

Among these traditional methods, ALD emerges as a particularly promising technology. ALD's unique nature enables high uniformity and easy composition and stacking process control. Recently, there has increased interest in individual layer fabrication processes, driven by continuous scale-down device needs. ALD operates on sequential self-limiting surface reaction principles, enabling atomic-scale film thickness and composition control. This unique feature allows extremely thin and conformal film deposition, essential for advanced semiconductor applications where dimensional control is critical.

Additionally, ALD provides superior high aspect ratio structure uniformity, overcoming one of PVD and CVD's major limitations. Despite slower deposition rates, ALD's precision and scalability position it as a key nano-scale device and structure development technology, highlighting its potential for driving future thin film technology innovations. Table 4 provides a comprehensive PVD, CVD, and ALD comparison.

PVD involves continuous growth mechanisms with 100 nm–1 μm min−1 growth rates, suitable for metals, elements, alloys, and some oxides/nitrides, operating at 20–500 °C with 10−6–10−3 torr vacuum requirements. It demonstrates line-of-sight directionality with particle cleanliness, 50–70% conformality, and ∼80 Å range uniformity.

CVD involves continuous growth mechanisms with 10–100 nm min−1 growth rates, suitable for oxides, nitrides, sulfides, and metals, operating at 200–1000 °C with 10−3–10 torr vacuum requirements. It demonstrates isotropic directionality with particle cleanliness, 70–90% conformality, and ∼10 Å range uniformity.

ALD involves self-limiting growth mechanisms with 0.1–1 nm min−1 growth rates, suitable for oxides, nitrides, sulfides, and metals, operating at 50–300 °C with 10−2–10 torr vacuum requirements. It demonstrates isotropic directionality with no particles, >90% conformality, and Å range uniformity.

### 3.2. Atomic Layer Deposition

Originally developed and globally recognized as atomic layer epitaxy in the late 1970s, ALD was initially motivated by the need to create thin-film electroluminescent (TFEL) flat-panel displays. These displays required high-quality dielectric and luminescent films on large-area substrates. By the mid-1980s, ALD utility was extended to epitaxial compound semiconductors. Significant research efforts in the late 1980s focused on III–V compound preparation. The renewed focus on ALD during the mid-1990s was driven by its silicon-based microelectronics applications. Continuous device dimension miniaturization and increasing integrated circuit aspect ratios demand novel materials and deposition techniques adoption. ALD is regarded as one of the most promising methods, owing to its ability to produce ultrathin, conformal films with precise atomic-level control over film thickness and composition. This section will further explore the ALD mechanism, area-selective atomic layer deposition (AS-ALD), and its applications.

#### 3.2.1. ALD Mechanism

ALD is a bottom-up method distinguished by its self-limiting reaction mechanisms, which ensure that chemical reactions of precursors and reactants on surfaces self-saturate once all initial reactive sites are occupied, independent of dosing amounts. This process involves complementary reactions, where at least two different molecules are separately exposed to substrates, facilitating thin film deposition. Such mechanisms enable conformal and high-quality thin film production at temperatures below 350 °C, achieving high uniformity even on substrates with high aspect ratios, a challenge remaining with conventional PVD methods.

Figure 15(a) presents a schematic comparison of sputtering and ALD systems, highlighting film deposition morphology differences on high-aspect-ratio devices. Figure 15(b) illustrates the general ALD process through cyclic surface reactions: (1) precursor A adsorption, (2) inert gas purging to remove excess precursors and byproducts, (3) precursor B adsorption, and (4) repeated purging to facilitate layer-by-layer growth until desired film thickness is achieved. This adsorption process involves physisorption and chemisorption, with excess physisorbed materials removable by inert gas purging. Active surface site limitations ensure each cycle surface reactions are self-limiting. Due to its self-limiting characteristics, ALD can precisely control film thickness at atomic levels by adjusting growth cycle numbers. The saturation process during each cycle restricts reactions to less than one atomic or molecular layer, promoting uniformity and excellent step coverage across complex surfaces and large areas.

ALD flexibility covers a wide material range, allowing oxides, nitrides, fluorides, sulfides, metals, polymers, and mixed or doped material deposition. It also supports multilayer deposition with customizable atomic level properties, expanding application scope. Notably, materials like Al₂O₃, TiO₂, and TiN can be deposited at temperatures below 150 °C, reducing temperature-sensitive substrate thermal damage risks.

Despite its advantages, ALD may exhibit undesirable side reactions, present challenges due to complex reaction chemistry and reactor design, and generally offer low throughput and potential conformality issues. To address low throughput issues, strategies such as spatial ALD and plasma-enhanced ALD (PE-ALD) have been developed. Spatial ALD increases deposition rates by simultaneously allowing continuous precursor exposure across different substrate regions, thereby bypassing ALD's traditional cyclic nature. In contrast, PE-ALD utilizes plasma to activate surface reactions, enabling faster deposition rates and facilitating less reactive surface deposition at lower temperatures. While these methods enhance throughput, they also introduce challenges including higher equipment costs and increased process complexity, which must be considered for industrial applications.

In general, the process temperature is crucial for ALD reactions. ALD must operate within a specific temperature range to avoid slow growth rates, thermal disintegration, and slow precursor reaction. The "temperature window" is defined as the range within which film growth reaches saturation. At temperatures below this window, reactions may be lacking or may lead to precursor condensation, while at higher temperatures, precursor desorption or thermal decomposition risk increases. Hence, maintaining conditions within the ALD temperature window is crucial for optimizing deposition processes.

As research continues to expand ALD capabilities and address limitations, this technique remains at the leading edge of thin film deposition technologies, promising further enhancements and wider applications in the future. This progress is essential for meeting evolving nano-engineering and microelectronics demands, where ALD provides increasingly critical atomic-level control.

#### 3.2.2. Area-Selective Atomic Layer Deposition (AS-ALD)

The advanced technique of AS-ALD is a crucial process in semiconductor device miniaturization. As semiconductor device feature sizes approach sub-5 nm, aligning features within multilayer stacks becomes a significant bottleneck. AS-ALD is distinguished by its capacity for precise feature alignment and thickness control within multilayer stacks, which is particularly crucial as devices transition from planar to three-dimensional structures.

This method enables selective growth on predefined substrate areas while leaving other regions unaffected. This significantly simplifies high-k dielectric layer alignment required for next-generation device scaling. The AS-ALD ability to control nucleation through surface chemistry modification reduces extensive lithography and etching step needs, minimizing expensive and hazardous reagent use. This technique notably improves fabrication process efficiency by utilizing the selectivity window—the number of cycles required before growth initiates in non-growth areas. The process is sensitive to non-growth area defects and impurities, which can inadvertently initiate ALD growth. Maintaining high purity levels in precursors, co-reactants, and purge gases is crucial for avoiding foreign species introduction that may affect surfaces during ALD processes.

Selectivity is determined not only by film thickness but it also serves as a key process efficiency indicator. As the growth phase exceeded the defined selective window, deposition began on non-growth regions, resulting in reduced selectivity. An additional etching step can be integrated into the conventional binary ALD cycle to address this issue. This step is crucial for removing nuclei in non-growth areas, thereby preserving high selectivity. Importantly, the etching process must be selectively defined to remove deposited material without affecting underlying substrate integrity.

This approach ensures precision and effectiveness of ALD techniques in applications requiring high pattern accuracy. For example, selective TiO₂ thin film deposition on Si/SiO₂-patterned surfaces has been demonstrated. The supercycle implementation enabled TiO₂ film deposition exceeding 12 nm while still maintaining 97% high selectivity. This study demonstrated advanced selective deposition technique capabilities, including ALD and ALE, among other self-limiting reaction processes. The findings make substantial contributions to understanding how complex atomic-scale structures can be engineered with precise material deposition, thereby enhancing these technologies' potential applications in semiconductor manufacturing and other fields.

Researchers further advanced AS-ALD by developing Hf₁₋ₓZrₓO₂ (HZO) thin film selective deposition methods through O₂ molecule catalytic dissociation. This significantly enhanced dielectric constants on metal substrates, achieving high dielectric constants of 34 on Ru and 31 on TiN substrates. XPS depth profiling illustrated selective nature and purity of deposited films. This study emphasizes potential of catalytic local activation to advance three-dimensional nanopatterning in nanoelectronic device fabrication.

Moreover, researchers utilized a rotary reactor to deposit TiN on submicrometer ZnO powder through both thermal and PE-ALD. This method proved effective in completely removing N(CH₃)₂ ligands. EDX mapping and TEM images confirmed uniform coatings on all powder agglomerates and individual particles. Their findings revealed that plasma-enhanced layers contained higher titanium and nitrogen content with significantly reduced oxygen and carbon levels compared to thermally grown films, indicating improved compositional purity. These studies collectively highlight AS-ALD effectiveness in achieving precise and selective thin-film deposition, critical for ongoing nanoscale device fabrication development. These findings explore their implications for semiconductor manufacturing futures, reflecting on how AS-ALD can address modern electronic device growing complexity and stringent requirements.

#### 3.2.3. ALD Applications

ALD capability to deposit conformal films with precise atomic thickness on complex surfaces is essential for interface modification, device encapsulation, and stabilizing quantum dots (QDs). In the semiconductor industry, it is important to enhance transistor efficiency by improving gate dielectric and interface quality. Moreover, ALD plays a crucial role in advancing photovoltaic technology by creating barrier layers that greatly enhance both efficiency and durability. Its utility further extends to catalyst production, where its precision in coating particles significantly enhances reactivity and selectivity, particularly in applications like fuel cells, chemical reactors, and environmental catalysis under various reaction conditions.

Especially in 3D IC fabrication, achieving conformal coating of high-aspect-ratio structures, such as through-silicon vias (TSVs), presents a significant challenge. ALD employs self-limiting surface reactions to deposit uniform and ultra-thin high-k dielectric films, which are essential for electrical insulation and capacitance optimization. ALE complements this process by providing precise TSV etching, ensuring minimal variability in depth and shape. Additionally, Neutral Beam Etching (NBE) enhances the overall process by minimizing plasma-induced damage, particularly in interconnect layers, thereby improving final device reliability.

##### 3.2.3.1. ALD in Optoelectronics Applications

As LED sizes decrease, sidewall defect impacts become more significant, resulting in reduced wafer luminous efficiency. ALD has become an important technology for improving LED device performance, especially for passivation applications. Sidewall passivation minimizes surface recombination velocities, which can greatly affect LED efficiency and performance.

A red micro-LED with SiO₂ by ALD used as a sidewall passivation layer is schematically shown with ALD offering superior coverage and conformality so that even the most complex microstructure LEDs are effectively passivated, reducing non-radiative sidewall recombination. From comparisons between devices with various coatings and different current levels, significant integrated optical power decrease can be observed. For smaller devices like those with 5 μm and 15 μm dimensions, ALD technology demonstrated lower leakage currents and higher conductivity compared to PECVD.

Specifically, in larger 50 μm devices, ALD-coated samples exhibited average leakage current reduction by a factor of 7.8, indicating ALD's greater sidewall passivation effectiveness, thereby minimizing leakage pathways and surface recombination losses. When examining various size micro-LEDs, ALD sidewall treatment significance becomes even more apparent. This significant difference is primarily attributed to increased surface recombination in smaller devices.

Micro-LEDs with ALD sidewall passivation demonstrate significantly higher output power densities and external quantum efficiencies (EQEs) compared to those without ALD treatment. The EQE enhancement in ALD sidewall passivation micro-LEDs is primarily due to Shockley–Read–Hall (SRH) non-radiative recombination reduction. The ALD treatment improves surface quality by passivating defects and reducing surface states, which are significant non-radiative recombination contributors, particularly in smaller devices. This leads to more efficient carrier recombination and higher light output. The uniform passivation ALD provides reduces surface states and defects, which are more pronounced in smaller devices due to their higher surface-to-volume ratios.

Consequently, ALD-treated micro-LEDs maintain higher efficiency and brightness, which is crucial for applications requiring high-resolution displays and precise lighting control. Specifically, ALD-treated devices show significant surface recombination velocity improvement, reducing it from 1026 cm s−1 to 551.3 cm s−1, which translates into 73.47% EQE enhancement for 5 μm devices and 66.72% for 10 μm devices.

Furthermore, ALD offers substantial advantages in LED and OLED device passivation and encapsulation. Its ability to produce high-quality, conformal films ensures enhanced current densities, output power, and EQE in micro-LEDs, as well as extended emission lifetimes in OLED panels. The EQE enhancement for ALD sidewall passivation micro-LEDs was significant, with improvements up to 73.47% for 5 μm devices and 66.72% for 10 μm devices. Additionally, ALD-treated OLED panels showed little to no luminance intensity degradation over extended periods, demonstrating ALD effectiveness in maintaining device performance.

##### 3.2.3.2. ALD in Microelectronics and Semiconductor Applications

As device dimensions decrease, conventional materials and fabrication methods encounter limitations, necessitating advanced solutions development like ALD. In FinFET cases, a 3D gate replaces planar grids, improving device density and performance. ALD enables advanced 3D gate structure uniform coating with high-k dielectrics like HfO2, achieving approximately 1 nm equivalent oxide thickness (EOT). This results in leakage current density reduction from approximately 10−6 A cm−2 to 10−9 A cm−2 and 85% universal SiO₂ mobility retention, significantly enhancing gate control, reducing leakage, and improving overall efficiency and scalability. Additionally, stress condition threshold voltage stability is maintained within ±5%, ensuring reliable device operation.

In microelectronics and semiconductors, ALD is pivotal for depositing high-k dielectric materials like hafnium oxide (HfO₂) and aluminum oxide (Al₂O₃). These high-k materials are characterized by higher capacitance density provision compared to traditional silicon dioxide, essential for transistor size scaling without increasing leakage currents. Specifically, HfO₂ and Al₂O₃ exhibit excellent insulating properties with high-k, which enhances gate capacitance while maintaining low equivalent oxide thickness. This results in improved gate control and reduced transistor power consumption. Moreover, their thermal stability and silicon process compatibility make them ideal for advanced semiconductor device use.

Figure 18(b) illustrates effective thermal conductivity of Al₂O₃, HfO₂, and TiO₂ films with 1 to 10 nm varying thicknesses, demonstrating their ability to maintain insulating properties across different scales, further contributing to reduced leakage currents and enhanced transistor performance. These high-k materials replace traditional silicon dioxide, reducing gate leakage currents and enhancing capacitance, thereby maintaining device performance at smaller scales.

In advanced memory devices like dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) and NAND flash, uniform trench structure requirements and precise capacitor fabrication is critical. For DRAM capacitors, ALD is essential for depositing ultra-thin, conformal HfO₂ layers, increasing capacitance density up to 30% while reducing leakage currents to 10−9 A cm−2. In NAND flash, ALD ensures uniform oxide–nitride–oxide (ONO) stack deposition, which significantly enhances memory retention and reliability, particularly in high-temperature environments. ALE further improves trench profiles, achieving 1 nm sidewall smoothness, essential for minimizing memory cell performance variability.

ALD facilitates high-k material deposition with exceptional uniformity, achieving 20–30% memory density increases and reducing leakage currents to levels below 10−8 A cm−2. ALE complements this process by creating precise trenches with minimal variability. Trenches fabricated via ALE exhibit depth uniformity exceeding 98%, which ensures consistent electrical performance and enhances capacitor reliability. Furthermore, ALD plays a crucial role in metal gate electrode formation. This transition from polysilicon gates to metal gates is essential for reducing gate resistance and enhancing transistor speed. Materials like titanium nitride (TiN) and tantalum nitride (TaN), deposited via ALD, ensure uniform complex 3D structure coverage, like fin structures in FinFETs. This uniformity forms effective gate electrodes that reduce gate resistance and thus improve overall transistor speed and efficiency.

In integrated circuits, ALD is vital for creating effective copper interconnect barrier and liner layers, preventing copper diffusion into surrounding materials, which is critical for circuit reliability. Figure 18(c) summarizes barrier and liner layer qualifying criteria, as well as surrounding materials and structure process-related issues. By reducing barrier/liner thickness to sub-nanometer levels, ALD enables 15% copper volume increase, thereby lowering line resistance and improving conductivity. Devices utilizing ALD-deposited barrier layers demonstrate 25% leakage current reduction, enhancing reliability and scalability for advanced nodes.

The conformity in high aspect ratio features is crucial for interconnect integrity, and ALD's precision is particularly effective here. For instance, ALD application in depositing semiconductor materials like indium gallium zinc oxide (IGZO) for thin-film transistors (TFTs) enables significant device performance improvements. IGZO deposited via ALD achieves higher electron mobility, typically exceeding 10 cm² V−1 s−1, compared to traditional amorphous silicon TFTs, which generally exhibit approximately 1 cm² V−1 s−1 mobilities. This mobility increase directly enhances advanced display technology switching speed and overall performance.

The uniform deposition capability ALD provides ensures consistent electrical properties across large areas and complex structures, critical for flexible and large-area electronics development. Furthermore, ALD's low-temperature deposition, often below 200 °C, preserves flexible and wearable electronics integrity by preventing sensitive substrate damage, which might occur at higher temperatures. For example, ZnO TFTs fabricated on plastic substrates using low-temperature ALD show high transparency (over 85%) and robust electrical performance, with on/off ratios exceeding 10⁶. This capability underscores ALD's potential in advancing high-performance, flexible electronic devices by maintaining material properties while ensuring mechanical flexibility.

Overall, ALD's role in semiconductor fabrication is expected to grow as smaller, more efficient electronic device demand increases. ALD's ability to deposit thin, uniform films with precise composition and thickness control makes it an essential tool for ongoing semiconductor technology innovations. Emerging applications, like 3D ICs and advanced memory devices, also benefit from ALD's precision and uniformity. In summary, ALD plays a crucial role in transistor technology advancement. For example, ALD ensures conformal TSV coating, achieving 95% vertical integration efficiency improvement, while ALE provides precise etching with minimal sidewall damage. These advancements support ongoing electronic device miniaturization and diversification.

In 3D ICs, ALD enables TSV high-k dielectric deposition for insulation, achieving leakage current reductions to levels below 10−8 A cm−2 and ensuring consistent capacitance values across high-aspect-ratio structures. This capability enhances interconnect reliability, reducing failure rates by over 20%. Additionally, ALE's atomic-scale precision allows TSV etching with depth uniformity exceeding 98%, critical for maintaining signal integrity in multi-layer chip designs.

##### 3.2.3.3. ALD in Environmental and Energy Domains

In addition to applications mentioned above, ALD is highly effective for depositing functional layers on specific surfaces. In photovoltaics, ALD enhances solar cell performance and durability by reducing surface recombination through rear silicon surface passivation layers. Typically made of Al₂O₃ or TiO₂, these layers decrease surface recombination velocities and increase efficiency. The incorporation of an ultrathin Al₂O₃ layer at the perovskite/PEDOT interface resulted in power conversion efficiency increase from 9.6% to 11.2%. This enhancement is attributed to charge recombination reduction and improved ALD layer passivation. Additionally, ALD-treated devices exhibited open-circuit voltage increase from 0.85 V to 0.91 V and fill factor improvement from 0.59 to 0.65.

The passivation mechanism involves chemical passivation, which reduces surface defect states, and field-effect passivation, which repels charge carriers away from surfaces. For example, Al₂O₃ layers introduce negative fixed charges that repel electrons, thereby reducing electron–hole recombination. ALD is also utilized in thin-film and perovskite solar cells to achieve precise layer thickness and composition control, optimizing device architecture and stability. This uniformity is essential for maintaining structural and functional solar cell integrity under environmental stressors like moisture and temperature variations.

Catalysis is another area where ALD has made substantial contributions. ALD enables catalytic material deposition with atomic precision, facilitating highly active and stable catalyst creation. For instance, ALD can deposit noble metal nanoparticles like platinum or palladium on high-surface-area supports, resulting in catalysts with high active site dispersion essential for catalytic reactions. A notable example involves atomically dispersed Pd on graphene fabrication using ALD. In selective 1,3-butadiene hydrogenation, the single-atom Pd₁/graphene catalyst demonstrated approximately 100% butene at 95% conversion. The high performance is attributed to 1,3-butadiene adsorption mode changes and enhanced steric effects on isolated Pd atoms.

Furthermore, ALD allows core–shell structure engineering, enhancing catalytic activity and selectivity, while also improving stability and resistance to sintering and poisoning. These attributes are particularly valuable in applications like automotive exhaust treatment, hydrogen production, and chemical synthesis.

In biomedicine fields, ALD's precise film property control has been harnessed for various applications. One prominent example is ALD use to create medical implant biocompatible coatings. These coatings, often made of materials like titanium dioxide or hydroxyapatite, enhance implant integration with biological tissues, reduce infection risks, and improve overall device biocompatibility. Additionally, ALD is utilized in drug delivery system development. The as-deposited ceramic films exhibited no cytotoxicity in the human cell culture environment. By coating nanoparticles with ALD films, it is possible to precisely control therapeutic agent release rates, enhance drug formulation stability, and target specific tissues or cells.

The implementation of ceramic ALD films like SiO₂, TiO₂, and Al₂O₃ significantly increased budesonide fine particle fractions. In the study, ceramic ALD film implementation on budesonide particles significantly increased the fine particle fraction (FPF) by approximately 2.3 times for SiO₂ and Al₂O₃ and 1.9 times for TiO₂, after 10 ALE cycles. This indicates high drug loading and improved cell viability. This precision is crucial for maximizing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing side effects.

Moreover, magnetic nanoparticles show promise in magnetic resonance imaging, hyperthermia treatment, and targeted drug delivery. The enhanced stability and biocompatibility provided by ALD coatings make these magnetic nanoparticles more effective for clinical applications.

## Conclusion

This comprehensive review has examined technological advancements, emerging materials, and processing techniques in advanced semiconductor fabrication. Atomic layer deposition, atomic layer etching, and neutral beam etching represent critical innovations enabling atomic-scale precision manufacturing necessary for next-generation semiconductor devices.

These advanced techniques address fundamental limitations of traditional methods, offering superior control over thin film growth, precise layer-by-layer material removal, and minimal surface damage preservation. Their combined application drives innovations in areas including micro-LEDs, optical communication, and high-frequency, high-power electronic devices.

ALD's self-limiting surface reactions enable conformal film deposition on complex high-aspect-ratio structures with exceptional uniformity, reducing leakage currents and enhancing device performance across microelectronics, optoelectronics, and energy applications. ALE's atomic precision removes material layer by layer, ideal for intricate nanoscale structure fabrication with minimal substrate damage. NBE's neutralized particle beams eliminate plasma-induced damage, particularly critical for GaN-based semiconductors requiring high reliability.

As semiconductor dimensions continue shrinking beyond conventional scaling, these atomic-scale technologies become increasingly indispensable. Their precision, control, and adaptability to diverse materials position them as enabling technologies for sustained semiconductor advancement, supporting continued innovation in device miniaturization, performance enhancement, and expanded functionality beyond traditional Moore's law predictions.
