ABSTRACT
With the increasing demand for devices in miniaturization, accuracy, and low power consumption, developing microdevices in the form of
piezoelectric thin films is significant for microelectromechanical systems (MEMS) applications. Piezoelectric thin films offer advantages of
miniaturization and low power consumption, holding immense potential in MEMS, especially with advancements in micro-nanomanufac-
turing technologies. In this review, we highlighted the compelling piezoelectric properties and summarized the latest research progress of
thin films, with an emphasis on recent advances in piezoelectric MEMS. We mainly introduced the recent developments on different types
of piezoelectric MEMS (piezo-MEMS), along with the descriptions of piezoelectric effects, film preparation, film properties, and device indi-
cators. We have emphasized the comparison of MEMS with different piezoelectric materials and methods for improving devices. The recent
achievements of piezoelectric thin films in MEMS applications and the future development of MEMS applications are also reviewed.

I. INTRODUCTION
In recent years, the rapid development of the Fifth Generation
Mobile Networks (5G) and the Internet of Things (IoT) relies
heavily on a plethora of miniaturized, low-cost, and low-power
devices, such as sensors and actuators. To meet the growing
demand for enhanced functionalities of microsensors and actuators,
there is a strong need for novel microdevices beyond traditional
Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS). Integrating functional
materials into MEMS devices has proven effective. Among various
functional materials, piezoelectric materials stand out as one of the
most suitable options. Even at the microscopic scale, piezoelectric
materials exhibit exceptional energy conversion efficiency between
mechanical and electrical energy.
Piezoelectric thin films have emerged as pivotal components
in the realm of Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems (MEMS) due to
their unique ability to convert mechanical energy into electrical
signals and vice versa. These thin films, typically composed of
materials like lead zirconate titanate (PZT) and aluminum nitride
(AlN), exhibit the piezoelectric effect, where they generate electric
charge in response to mechanical stress. In MEMS applications,
this property enables precise actuation and sensing capabilities,

This review provides a brief introduction and overview of
various preparation techniques for different piezoelectric thin films
used in MEMS, MEMS processing procedures, and thin film testing
methods. The second section covers various techniques for fabricat-
ing piezoelectric thin films, photolithography and etching pro-
cesses, as well as encapsulation and testing methods. Additionally,
it delves into several classic applications of MEMS devices in the
realms of actuators, energy harvesters, and sensors. Due to the vast
interdisciplinary nature of piezoelectric MEMS, we have chosen to
focus on introducing recent years’ most popular and widely applied
MEMS devices primarily based on thin-film processes from the
perspectives of materials and applications. Subsequently, we will
present MEMS acoustic sensors utilizing piezoelectric thin films,
with piezoelectric micro-mechanical ultrasonic transducers and
piezoelectric microphones as the main representative devices.
Following that, different materials will be sequentially introduced
to analyze and compare the design principles and output perfor-
mance of piezoelectric MEMS energy harvesters. Given the rapid
advancement in optical and imaging technologies, an analysis and
comparison of piezoelectric MEMS actuators for micro-mirror
devices will be conducted. Finally, the status quo of piezoelectric
MEMS devices was summarized, highlighting key challenges that
need resolution to enable practical application devices and outlin-
ing future prospects.
II. MEMS PROCESSING OF PIEZOELECTRIC THIN FILMS
There are three approaches to realize piezoelectric MEMS
devices: (i) depositing piezoelectric thin films on silicon substrates
with appropriate insulating and device layers, followed by surface
micromachining to achieve microfabricated transducer devices
(additive approach). (ii) Micromachining bulk piezoelectric materi-
als such as single crystals and piezoelectric ceramics to alter the
shape and structure of the bulk material for microfabricated trans-
ducer devices (subtractive approach). (iii) Integrating silicon-based
microstructures onto bulk piezoelectric or silicon substrates using
wafer bonding techniques (integrative approach). A comparison
based on MEMS dimensions, piezoelectric properties, and integra-
tion techniques is shown in Fig. 1. Piezoelectric thin films with
thickness ranging from 100 nm to several tens of micrometers
exhibit piezoelectric performance close to that of corresponding
bulk materials. Figure 1 illustrates that thin films have significant
advantages over the other two approaches in terms of integrated
production and overall performance. Therefore, Secs. II A–II C will
focus on the processing of thin films in MEMS. Thin film MEMS
processing can be roughly categorized as thin film preparation,
etching, and packaging testing.
A. Piezoelectric thin film deposition
Piezoelectric thin films can be deposited using various tech-
niques such as sputtering, chemical solution methods, and atomic
layer deposition. Generally, all of these methods involve three main
steps: (i) the prepared thin film structure can be categorized as
single-crystalline, randomly polycrystalline, or highly oriented poly-
crystalline. (ii) The deposition of the thin film requires a corre-
sponding substrate [e.g., Si (100), MgO, SrTiO3(100), mica, nickel
sheet, Al2O3]. (iii) The temperature for thin film deposition and
annealing. Based on how atomic or molecular substances are gener-
ated and brought to the substrate, these methods can be broadly
classified into chemical methods (e.g., chemical vapor deposition,
sol–gel techniques, etc.) or physical methods (e.g., sputtering,
molecular beam epitaxy, and pulsed laser deposition). Several
methods have been explored for each piezoelectric material of
interest, each with varying degrees of success and advantages.
Currently, one of the most critical challenges in the commercializa-
tion of MEMS is how to achieve large-scale, large-area production
of thin films while ensuring high yield of the thin film products.

B. Etching thin films
The etching technique typically employs wet etching and dry
etching. A graphic method with affordable alternatives, strong
selectivity, and somewhat quick etching rates is wet etching, but it
is characterized by low precision in etching microstructures, espe-
cially for wet etching of PZT thin films with the low precision of
etching and fluoride residues. Wang and co-workers25 applied a
wet etching technique for PZT to enhance the functionality of
scanning mirrors. Wet etching is frequently employed in MEMS
and optoelectronic devices; because it is only isotropic, it cannot
precisely regulate the etching of the device shape below 2μm. Most
well-known piezoelectric materials can undergo wet etching in
strong acids and bases. For instance, quartz crystals have been
found to dissolve rapidly in NaOH and KOH solutions.26,27

Controlled etching of ZnO requires the use of weak acids with
appropriate ion levels. Various acids and bases such as HCl,

become an effective and popular technology in MEMS due to its
high etching rate, low cost, low surface damage, and high
selectivity.32–34 There is currently no effective wet etching method
for KNN films, as KNN remains stable in both acidic and alkaline
environments.
Ion beam etching is a sort of dry etching that can be used to
precisely etch ultra-thin films on materials that are challenging to
etch chemically. Any known substance can be etched using it as
well. Ion beam etching has anisotropy, which exists in a high aspect
ratio of directionality and high resolution. As a result, it is also uti-
lized to process the microstructure of PZT ferroelectric thin films,
but the outstanding limitation of the method is high cost and
damage to thin films caused by high temperatures. When selecting
gas species for plasma etching, the primary consideration depends
on the formation of volatile byproducts as part of the decomposi-
tion of the metal oxide being etched. Typically, in dry etching of
AlN, chlorine gas is used as the etching gas, with Ti/Cr used as the
mask layer and top electrode. Yang et al.35 reported a dry etching
method using Cl2/BCl3/Ar at 700 W power and 0.5 Pa pressure.
They employed SiO2 as the mask layer and Pt as the bottom elec-
trode acting as an etch stop layer. The selectivity of AlN/Pt was 3:1.
Although dry etching is highly anisotropic and can produce vertical
profiles, it is not preferred as highly reactive Cl2 plasma exhibits
lower etching selectivity between materials and may potentially
cause damage through ion bombardment. For patterning PZT thin
films, dry etching techniques are commonly used. It generates pat-
terns with minimal undercutting. However, due to differences in
the sputtering efficiency of Pb, Zr, and Ti, it is challenging to
achieve satisfactory PZT patterns. To address the limitation of dry
etching in effectively patterning satisfactory features, a method
involving a mixture of various plasma gases is chosen to enhance
the etching depth. A comparison of wet and dry etching character-
istics for different piezoelectric thin film materials is presented in

devices, which is the key to practicality and commercialization.
However, the dependability and long-term stability of MEMS
devices can be severely impacted by residual stresses induced
during the packaging process, which can lead to shifts in the
output signals of MEMS devices. As a result, achieving excellent
performance and stability of MEMS devices requires decreasing
package stress.44–48 According to studies, the most significant cause
of stress in chip-scale packaging is the chip bonding procedure. In
particular, large package stresses are produced by the silicon
MEMS device substrate’s material coefficient of thermal expansion
(CTE) mismatch with the package substrate. For resonant devices
or pressure-sensitive devices, such as MEMS resonators, accelerom-
eters, and gyroscopes, which undergo geometrical deformation as a
result of package stresses, the device’s resonant frequency can
change,

inaccuracies
in
the

output
signal.

technique and bonding materials to reduce encapsulation stress.
Authors such as Braeuer et al. have demonstrated the viability of
the reactive bonding approach using extremely thin multilayer
films of Ti/a-Si and Al/Pd. A candidate for further research in reac-
tive bonding, such as for MEMS packaging, is the highly energetic
Al/Pd system.49 Lee et al. employed a glass–silicon anodic bonding
technique in an innovative vacuum packaging process, which sig-
nificantly improved the yield of vacuum packages. Notably, no
vacuum degradation was observed even after 1000 h of testing.50
The development of new packaging materials, which should be
dedicated to the creation of universally beneficial low-stress
bonding materials and packaging substrate materials that perfectly
match the CTE of the MEMS materials, is the main strategy for
reducing encapsulation stress in the future. The second strategy is
the continued optimization of the encapsulation structure.

(PMUT)
PMUT is also a common type of piezoelectric MEMS micro-
sensors. When operating in receive mode, the PMUT is used to
detect ultrasonic waves. When the ultrasonic signal acts on the

generated inside the piezoelectric film, which drives the film to
vibrate up and down, and the internal charge changes, and the
upper and lower surfaces of the film will generate charge signals
with different polarity, which are extracted by the upper and lower
electrodes of the film to achieve the conversion of mechanical
energy to electrical energy. The change of charge signal is extracted
by the upper and lower electrodes of the film to achieve the conver-

From a materials perspective, PZT is a widely used piezoelec-
tric material applied in PMUTs. Due to its very high piezoelectric
coefficient (|e31,f| ∼12 C/m2), PZT is ideal for transmitter applica-
tions. However, the high dielectric constant of PZT (ε33,f ∼1200)
makes it unsuitable for use as a receiver. In 1990, Udayakumar
et al.72 employed sol–gel-deposited PZT thin films for bending
wave applications. Subsequently, in 1996, Muralt et al.73 demon-
strated the effects of PZT thin films deposited by magnetron sput-
tering on the membrane structure and electrical parameters of
PMUTs. AlN is a complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor
(CMOS)-compatible material that allows for cost-effective mass
production. Its lower ε33,f makes AlN well-suited for PMUT receiv-
ers. Shelton et al.74 first proposed the use of aluminum nitride
(AlN) thin films as an active layer in PMUTs, thereby creating
PMUT arrays compatible with CMOS technology. Furthermore, the
toxicity of lead restricts its use in medical-related applications,
making the development of lead-free PMUTs significant. KNN
materials exhibit piezoelectric performance comparable to PZT
while remaining environmentally friendly. In 2022, Huang et al.75
successfully fabricated PMUT devices based on KNN thin films on
2-in. SOI wafers, pioneering the use of environmentally friendly
lead-free ferroelectric materials in PMUTs. These devices achieved
a super high transmittance sensitivity of 1250 nm/V at 66.2 kHz,
surpassing most commercially available PZT-based PMUTs.
From an application perspective, piezoelectric thin-film ultra-
sound imaging offers advantages over traditional ultrasonic trans-
ducers (e.g., bulk piezoelectric ceramics) due to its low acoustic
impedance, enabling good coupling with air and liquids without
the need for additional matching layers. The ultrasound imaging
capabilities of PMUTs have become a hot topic. Bernstein et al.76
fabricated PZT thin film PMUT arrays using sol–gel deposition for
underwater ultrasound imaging. Yamashita et al.77 demonstrated
airborne 3D imaging using sol–gel-derived thin film PZT-PMUT
for the first time. Recently, PMUTs have been widely applied in
low-cost, portable medical imaging and non-destructive testing of
complex microstructures. Dausch et al.78 demonstrated real-time
intracardiac scanning using PZT-PMUT fabricated via sol–gel dep-
osition, showcasing primary in vivo medical ultrasound imaging
applications. Wang et al.79 proposed an application of an 18 MHz
PZT-based PMUT with a polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) waveguide
for imaging. Ji et al.80 demonstrated the imaging capability of
PMUTs with PZT/Si membrane structures for solid defect detec-
tion. Lu et al. developed a short-range AlN-PMUT high-resolution
ultrasound imaging system with a focused beam of 90 μm in diam-
eter.81 On the other hand, ultrasound fingerprint sensing offers sig-
nificant advantages, such as being less affected by wet or
contaminated fingers. In 2010, Liu and co-workers82 introduced a
handwriting input system using PZT-based PMUTs. Tang et al.83
reported an ultrasound fingerprint sensor composed of 110 × 56
matrix elements using AlN PMUTs with a spacing of 43 μm. A pol-

PMUTs have been widely employed for flow measurements in
various scenarios involving gases, water, oil, and blood. Their
advantages lie in their ability to perform non-destructive testing
and detect complex channel structures. In gas measurement appli-
cations, Chen et al.85 developed a high-precision airflow volume
flowmeter using a time-of-flight-based PMUT array. Two 10 × 10
arrays of multiple AlN PMUTs with a resonant frequency of
980 kHz alternately transmit and receive ultrasonic signals.
For liquid measurements, an AlN-PMUT array installed exter-

a 3.7 MHz PMUT system based on piezoelectric AlN thin films for
wearable blood flow measurements using pulse wave Doppler
(PWD) ultrasound. According to the Doppler effect, both the
average Doppler frequency and Doppler power spectral density
exhibit linear changes relative to blood flow rate.
B. Piezoelectric microphones
MEMS piezoelectric microphones are a class of miniature
devices that combine acoustic sensors with pressure-sensitive dia-
phragms and dedicated integrated circuits (ASICs), manufactured
on silicon wafers to sense sound and audio. Compared to tradi-
tional electret condenser microphones, MEMS piezoelectric micro-
phones offer higher performance density, compatibility with
micrometer-scale circuits, temperature stability, low power con-
sumption, low losses, and lower vibration sensitivity. With the
development of miniaturization, wearable, portable devices, and
thin-film technologies, the application of MEMS piezoelectric
microphones has garnered widespread attention.
From a materials perspective, the piezoelectric coefficient of
zinc oxide (ZnO) is approximately −5 pC/N, indicating that
ZnO-based acoustic sensors are more sensitive and are becoming
popular materials for the design of acoustic sensors and acousto-
electric devices. Royer et al.88 first realized a ZnO piezoelectric
microphone, achieving a sensitivity of 0.25 mV/Pa on a 30 μm
thick ZnO film. Ali et al.89 designed a ZnO thin-film piezoelectric
silicon microelectromechanical system (MEMS) acoustic sensor,
achieving high sound pressure levels of up to ∼180 dB. Increasing
the decibels and operating bandwidth is a method to enhance the
performance of sound sensors. Arora et al.90 achieved enhanced
sensitivity by depositing piezoelectric ZnO thin films using sputter-
ing techniques. They utilized pressure compensation channels in
MEMS for pressure compensation, achieving different sound pres-
sure levels (SPLs) ranging from 120 to 180 dB. Sang et al.91 also
reported a device that can function as both a microphone and a
microspeaker. It features a 0.5 μm thick ZnO piezoelectric film
made using chemical vapor deposition. The measured microphone
sensitivity is quite stable in the low-frequency range, at 30 mV/Pa,
rising to 200 mV/Pa at the lowest resonant frequency of 890 Hz.
Aluminum nitride (AlN) possesses high dielectric constant,
resistivity, piezoelectricity, thermal conductivity, and chemical
stability, offering better chemical and thermal stability compared to
ZnO films. Williams et al.92 designed an AlN microphone capable
of measuring a sensitivity of −39 μV/Pa, a minimum detectable

From an application perspective, piezoelectric microphones
can be utilized for locating carrier rockets and detecting aircraft
noise to restrict aircraft noise emissions. Horowitz co-workers96
were the first to use PZT thin-film microphones for aerodynamic
acoustics measurement applications. Williams et al.92 designed an
AlN microphone to aid in identifying aircraft noise sources and/or
evaluating the effectiveness of noise reduction technologies. In the
field of biomedical monitoring, they are used to monitor various
biological signals, such as the respiratory sounds of patients with
sleep apnea and chest sounds in neonatal care units. Surface acous-
tic wave interdigital transducer (SAW-IDT) sensors are developed
for measurement, typically using air-coupled microphones and/or
lightweight accelerometers attached to the skin.97 MEMS-based pie-
zoelectric microphones are suitable for wearable health monitoring
devices, smart hearing aids, and implantable medical devices.
Piezoelectric microphones can be applied as cochlear implants
(CIs) in biomedical systems. Zargarpour and Zarifi et al.98 designed
a piezoelectric microelectromechanical microphone for implantable
hearing aid applications using AlN as the material. They designed
it based on the simulation of cochlear structures for hearing aids
and other medical devices. Zhao et al.99 designed a biocompatible
cochlear implant for long-term in vivo studies in guinea pigs using
AlN thin films. Another interesting application area for hydropho-
bic microphones is pipeline leak detection, ensuring automatic
monitoring of pipeline pressure even in remote locations to guaran-
tee rapid detection and correction.100

VI. ENERGY HARVESTERS OF PIEZOELECTRIC THIN
FILMS
With the flourishing development of various devices and func-
tional systems in the Internet of Things (IoT), distributed and self-
sustaining power sources have become a bottleneck limiting further
advancements in 5G and IoT. In comparison to traditional power
sources such as batteries, which are large, heavy, require frequent
replacement after depletion, have low power capacity, and poor com-
patibility with current miniaturized devices, energy harvesting tech-
nologies provide a promising solution for achieving self-sustaining
devices and systems. MEMS-based piezoelectric thin-film energy
harvesters offer small form factors, convenient configurations,
compatibility with microfabrication processes, power output in the
microwatt range, and low losses. They can be applied in wearable
devices,
implantable
biomedical
devices,
self-powered
energy
systems, and devices for indoor and outdoor use in the future.

energy

demonstrate
significant
potential for integration with miniaturized IoT devices and systems.

through thin-film deposition.

In the manufacturing and sizing of piezoelectric actuators, two
approaches can be distinguished: (i) traditional machining methods
involve bonding ceramic blocks/single crystals to optical compo-

manner are characterized by large dimensions, stable driving force,
and high costs. (ii) Silicon-based microfabrication techniques are
combined with thin film deposition. Devices manufactured using
this method are characterized by miniaturization, short response
times, and low costs, making them suitable for precision optical
components. This section mainly focuses on piezoelectric scanning
mirrors and deformable mirrors (DMs) primarily based on piezo-
electric thin films.
The development and deployment of Adaptive Optics (AO)
are related to the challenge of reliable microdevices for compensat-
ing laser beam aberrations.139–141 Wavefront correctors are crucial
components of any AO system. The rapid advancement of
piezoelectric-driven AO technology has played a significant role in
fields such as astronomical imaging, visual sciences, and high-
energy lasers. The purpose of a Piezoelectric DM is to correct
shape errors associated with component manufacturing or atmo-
spheric distortions.
A special application of integrating PZT thin films with
control electronics is in the adjustable optical components of the
next-generation x-ray telescopes. To enhance the collecting area of
the telescope while achieving high angular resolution, a significant
amount of electrically connected control electronics is required.
However, the current incompatibility between PZT-based MEMS and
existing CMOS arises from the fact that the crystallization temperature
of PZT is often >500 °C, causing dopant distribution in integrated cir-
cuits to become mobile and aluminum contacts to potentially oxidize
at such temperatures. Additionally, passivation of electronic devices
typically introduces hydrogen, thereby compromising the performance
of piezoelectric devices. Wallace et al.142 developed a piezoelectric
deformable mirror that integrates ZnO control electronics with PZT,
ensuring good compatibility. Other researchers have focused on
improving DM pixel performance based on the strain and hysteresis
of PZT. Huisman et al.143 utilized the large hysteresis in piezoelectric
actuators combined with pixel layout in a DM based on Pb(Nb, Zr,
Ti)O3 (PNZT) thin films to overcome typical issues such as pixel
drift, manufacturing, and reliability when considering high pixel
counts and pixel densities in DMs. MEMS piezoelectric DMs can be
classified based on the distribution of backside electrode and piezo-
electric unit modules into: (i) trapezoidal distributed and (ii) hexago-
nal distributed. This mainstream distribution is designed to ensure
that the mirror does not fracture under deformation stress. AlN-based
piezoelectric thin films can be deposited at low temperatures without
requiring additional polarization treatments, promoting excellent
CMOS compatibility, and can be easily patterned using Cl2-based dry
etching methods. Bi-directional linear and non-hysteretic driving,
along with good optical aberrations, is achieved. Zhang et al.144 pro-
posed a variable mirror based on AlN trapezoidal distribution.
Within a driving voltage range of 100 V, the AlN DM can achieve a
maximum peak-to-valley (PV) defocus of 19.81 μm and a maximum
PV spherical aberration of 7.63 μm. DMs can also be applied to
retinal sensors to achieve stable optical images through optical aberra-
tion correction.145,146 Table VII illustrates a comparison of different

modulate the propagating light. Such devices could become core
components for future classical and quantum hybrid signal pro-
cessing, networking, and computing. The high piezoelectric and
electro-optic coupling properties of LiNbO3 provide the modulator
with higher microwave-to-optical conversion efficiency. Therefore,
Shao et al.163 utilized driven piezoelectric acoustic resonators fabri-
cated on suspended thin-film LiNbO3 to modulate light propagat-

in

(MZIs)

has also been validated as a low-loss high-quality optical material

strain-induced modulation. Ghosh et al.165 employed an AlN-based

Optical metasurfaces (OMSs) stand as novel optical modula-
tors capable of unprecedented manipulation of light’s phase, ampli-
tude, and polarization. The advancement of micro-nano processing
technology has further propelled the potential applications of OMS
in display, imaging, sensing, anti-counterfeiting, and light field reg-
ulation. MEMS-based mechanical control of metasurfaces repre-
sents one of the research directions in dynamically tunable OMS.
By varying the voltage applied to MEMS, the angle of cantilevers
can be adjusted to achieve distinct optical responses, primarily reg-
ulating the phase, amplitude, and polarization of light. Regarding
optical phase modulation, the MEMS-OMS platform offers a
compact and efficient alternative to bulky and energy-intensive
refractive (curved) lenses and stepper motors. Dirdal et al.168 devel-
oped a MEMS-adjustable dielectric metasurface lens driven by PZT
films, achieving large displacements at low voltages in a compact
form. This metasurface zoom lens could substitute traditional
lenses in personalized medical diagnostics, drone-based sensing
and imaging (e.g., environmental monitoring), and wearable
devices (e.g., augmented reality glasses, smartwatches, mobile
phones).
Regarding optical amplitude modulation, Meng et al.169 inte-
grated PZT film piezoelectric MEMS with gap surface plasmon
(GSP)-based optical metasurfaces, creating a piezoelectric-driven
dynamic MEMS-OMS platform for efficient, broadband, and fast
reflective 2D wavefront shaping. This platform provides a flexible
solution for complex dynamic two-dimensional wavefront opera-
tions, suitable for reconfigurable and adaptive optical networks and
systems. Regarding polarization control, the Meng et al.170 team
combined plasmonic OMS with carefully designed anisotropic
subunits and thin-film piezoelectric MEMS mirrors, achieving high
polarization conversion efficiency (around 75%), broadband opera-
tion (approximately 100 nm near an 800 nm working wavelength),
rapid response (<0.4 ms), and comprehensive birefringence control.
This achievement facilitates complete electrical control of light
polarization, paving new pathways for further integration and mini-
aturization of optical networks and systems. OMS-based polariza-
tion applications extend to information encryption and image
anti-counterfeiting. Deng et al.171 demonstrated a MEMS-OMS-
based dynamic linear polarizer (DLP) with an electrically con-
trolled extinction ratio, allowing rapid and reversible adjustments
by combining anisotropic plasmonic OMS with thin-film piezoelec-
tric MEMS mirrors. This design enables adaptive photonic systems
for advanced applications in optical image encryption and display
fields.
VIII. PIEZOELECTRIC MEMS RESONATORS
In the era of 5 G/IoT, the emergence of a large number of con-
sumer electronics products with wireless and mobile communica-
tion capabilities has increased the demand for RF front-end
components. Piezoelectric MEMS resonators, as the main building
blocks of the RF front-end (filters and oscillators), play a crucial
role in frequency control and precise timing. The filter industry has
traditionally been divided into two main technological paths:
surface acoustic wave filters for lower frequency bands and bulk
acoustic wave filters for higher frequency bands. To meet the
requirements of 5G applications for frequencies below 6 GHz and
to provide RF filters with wider absolute bandwidth and relative
bandwidth, research progress on resonators, particularly focusing
on improvements in thin film materials and structures, is discussed
in this section.
From a material perspective, PZT exhibits high piezoelectric
coefficients and large dielectric constants, with its physical con-
stants being an active function of the applied DC electric field. The
large piezoelectric coefficient allows for the design of resonators
and filters with low terminal impedances, helping to minimize
impedance mismatch losses in the system. Pulskamp et al.172 dem-
onstrated significant improvements in out-of-band suppression
(>35 dB) with PZT-based RF MEMS resonators, consistent with

acteristics are (i) effective electromechanical coupling coefficient
(k2
eff) and (ii) quality factor (Q). Introducing other materials into
the resonator is also an effective way to enhance the device’s elec-

films as massless top electrodes for AlN NEMS resonators, driven
by platinum bottom interdigital transducer (IDT) electrodes. This
unique combination led to improved k2
eff and higher resonant fre-

of the resonator is primarily limited by anchor losses caused by
mechanical energy leakage and interface losses due to uneven stress
distribution between the metal electrode layer and the piezoelectric
thin film, which can be enhanced by optimizing the structure.
Wang et al.177 adopted a unique approach to enhance the Q value
by placing cylindrical air holes (micro-cavities) in a square array
between the device IDTs, forming a Phononic Crystal (PnC) reso-
nator to store mechanical energy within the micro-cavities.
Sun et al.178 designed a grid-shaped diamond electrode array to
excite two-dimensional Lamb waves (along orthogonal directions)
in the AlN layer. By optimizing the device structure and introduc-
ing new piezoelectric materials and different thin film materials as

IX. SUMMARY AND PERSPECTIVES
With the rapid advancement of the fifth-generation mobile
networks (5G) and the Internet of Things (IoT), the escalating
demand for a multitude of downsized, cost-effective, and low-
power sensors and actuators urgently necessitates new microdevices
MEMS surpassing traditional micro-electro-mechanical systems.
Integrating functional materials into MEMS devices has proven to
be effective. Presently, MEMS devices have evolved toward integra-
tion and miniaturization (e.g., PMUT integrated arrays), multifunc-
tionality (developing piezoelectric thin films with multifunctional
properties enabling simultaneous sensing, actuation, and energy
conversion), self-powering systems (e.g., wearable gloves, shoes, and
clothing serving as functional detection systems), nanotechnology,
and material innovations (manufacturing more efficient piezoelec-
tric thin films to enhance their performance and stability).
However, MEMS devices still exhibit shortcomings. First,
many piezoelectric devices have excessively narrow operating band-
widths (e.g., numerous energy harvesters have narrow operating
frequencies, operating either below or above the natural environ-
mental frequency for acceleration and power). Second, there are
issues with the compatibility of piezoelectric materials and device
substrates (e.g., poor compatibility of PZT with silicon-based sub-
strates). Lastly, the immaturity of thin-film material processes, espe-
cially in lead-free material systems with complex compositions,
presents challenges such as low reproducibility and poor film uni-
formity, requiring resolution.
This paper reviews the current research status of MEMS based
on piezoelectric thin films, emphasizing device miniaturization and
thin-film process technology development. It outlines piezoelectric
thin film materials for MEMS, including PZT, AlN, KNN, ZnO
piezoelectric materials. Additionally, it lists various devices such as
