## Abstract
Extreme ultraviolet lithography represents a leading semiconductor manufacturing technology for creating high-resolution patterns. This review examines recent developments in inorganic metal-oxide photoresists for EUVL applications. The study focuses on zinc-based, tin-oxygen, and IVB group inorganic photoresists, highlighting their enhanced chemical reactivity and precise patterning capabilities. Key advancements include zinc oxide and tin oxide nanoparticles that demonstrate improved photon absorption and solubility under extreme ultraviolet exposure. The review explores photochemical reactions of tin-oxygen clusters and ligand effects on film density and cross-linking. Findings indicate that inorganic photoresists improve photolithographic performance while offering potential for applications including pyroelectric infrared sensors and 3D printing. Future research directions include optimizing process parameters, exploring novel ligand and metal combinations, and evaluating environmental benefits of inorganic versus traditional organic photoresists. These advancements are expected to enhance resolution and patterning capabilities for next-generation semiconductor devices.

## Key Points
- Reviews three families of metal-oxide EUV photoresists: zinc-based, tin-oxygen (Sn-oxo clusters), and IVB group
- Metal-oxide resists offer higher photon absorption cross-section than traditional CARs at 13.5 nm
- Tin-oxo clusters are leading candidates, with tunable ligand chemistry affecting film properties
- Discusses photochemical mechanisms: EUV photon absorption, secondary electron generation, cross-linking
- Addresses environmental benefits of inorganic resists vs. organic chemically amplified resists

This review provides detailed coverage of next-generation EUV resist materials (metal oxide resists), which are expected to replace chemically amplified resists at advanced nodes — a critical topic for the v2 optical lithography chapter.

# Recent Advances in Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresists for EUV Lithography

## 1. Introduction

Extreme ultraviolet lithography (EUVL) has emerged as the leading technology for semiconductor manufacturing since 2019, succeeding deep ultraviolet lithography. The progression from visible light (436 nm) through g-line and i-line wavelengths to current EUV at 13.5 nm demonstrates how shorter wavelengths enable creation of smaller nanostructures per Rayleigh's criterion.

A critical challenge in EUVL involves balancing two requirements: high transmissivity to minimize line edge roughness (LER) and line width roughness (LWR), alongside high EUV light absorption to maximize incoming radiation utilization. Research has confirmed that metal oxides absorb EUV light substantially more efficiently than organic polymers—tin-oxide photoresists demonstrated absorption rates two to three times greater than polymer-based alternatives.

The elevated photon energy in EUV (92 eV) creates both advantages and challenges. While high-energy photons generate secondary electrons that cascade through materials initiating cross-linking and chemical reactions, they also create a paradox: fewer photons constitute the same energy dose compared to DUV, making it harder to activate necessary chemical reactions in traditional chemically amplified photoresists (CARs).

Metal-oxide-based photoresists offer distinct benefits: superior EUV absorptivity, high etch resistance, enhanced sensitivity through higher reactive species density, and formation of highly cross-linked inorganic networks providing mechanical and thermal stability. Inpria Corporation demonstrated this potential by achieving 13 nm half-pitch resolution using tin-oxide-based resists.

## 2. Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresist Chemistry

### 2.1 Zinc-Based Inorganic Photoresist Mechanism

Zinc-based photoresists incorporate zinc oxide (ZnO) as the photoactive component, combined with binders—either metal oxides like alumina/silica or organic polymers such as polyacrylic acid. The system includes solvents, additives, and potentially photoinitiators to enhance photochemical reactions.

The metal oxo core, densely packed with zinc atoms, is surrounded by trifluoroacetate and methacrylate ligands. These ligands enhance solubility and increase EUV photon absorption through fluorine content. Formation of zinc oxide clusters occurs through hydrolysis-dehydration reactions.

Upon light exposure, ZnO nanoparticles absorb photons, generating reactive species including free radicals, electrons, or holes. These reactive species initiate polymerization, cross-linking, or degradation reactions. Critical to performance is the extent of EUV light absorption, determined largely by ligand composition and metal density.

### 2.2 Tin-Oxygen Inorganic Photoresist Mechanism

Tin-oxygen photoresists comprise tin oxide (SnO2) nanoparticles in solution, arranged as tin oxo clusters. The RSn₁₂O₁₄OH₆X₂ structure features twelve tin atoms in a spherical cage, each bonded to an organic group, with the clusters carrying a bivalent charge typically associated with anion pairs.

The photochemical mechanism involves photodecomposition and cross-linking initiated by EUV exposure. The primary reaction is homolytic cleavage of the tin-carbon (Sn-C) bond (approximately 50 kcal/mol), producing tin-centered radicals that initiate inter-cluster reactions and cross-linking. The carboxylate counterions act as non-reactive spacers, with larger counterions increasing inter-cluster spacing and reducing sensitivity.

Studies using hard X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy revealed that the lowest electronically excited triplet state involves σ → d transitions where the Sn-C bond readily splits. Resulting tin radicals react with oxygen and water, increasing tin oxidation states. Butyl radicals donate hydrogen atoms or abstract them from neighboring cages, forming volatile molecules like butane or butene that escape easily. Post-exposure baking enhances development of insoluble material through formation of oxidation-sensitive but otherwise stable reaction products.

### 2.3 IVB Group Inorganic Photoresist Mechanism

Hafnium oxide and zirconium oxide photoresists enhance etch resistance and resolution through reduced organic content and careful ligand selection. Reducing organic content in hafnium oxide photoresists improves etch resistance by 2-3 times.

Research on zirconium oxide (ZrOx), titanium oxide (TiOx), and methacrylic acid (MAA) composites using STEM and XPS analysis revealed that ZrOx cores showed better dispersibility than TiOx cores, attributed to stronger interactions between ZrOx cores and shell molecules. XPS analysis confirmed that ZrOx-MAA photoresists experienced partial decomposition of surface ligand molecules after exposure, increasing electron density of zirconium atoms.

UV illumination causes minor ligand dissociation from metal particle surfaces, altering surface charge and causing aggregation, increased particle size, and solubility changes. HfO₂ modified with MAA and DMA ligands was more prone to agglomeration than versions with benzoic acid ligands due to stronger BA-particle interactions creating more stable formations.

Grazing incidence X-ray scattering analysis revealed that particles showed minimal aggregation, indicating that solubility changes may result from coupling of terminal C-C double bonds rather than significant ligand loss or extensive polymerization.

### 2.4 Absorptivity Comparison Under EUV

At EUV wavelengths (13.5 nm), EUV light interaction with electronic states is considerably stronger than DUV-molecular interaction. Higher lithographic resolution requires thinner coatings (approximately 35 nm) for EUV compared to DUV to prevent pattern collapse. However, a typical organic photoresist film of this thickness, with absorption of 4.8 μm⁻¹, utilizes only about 15% of incident light—a critical consideration given that generating EUV light is expensive and EUV sources typically have around 5% efficiency with limited collector lifetimes.

EUV light absorption depends entirely on atomic composition and element absorption cross-sections. Transition metals and metals/semimetals with high atomic numbers exhibit deep semicore orbitals with large interaction cross-sections with EUV photons, making them promising candidates for enhancing photoresist absorption.

Experimental studies measuring photoresist absorption coefficients revealed significant differences. Tin-based compounds exhibited coefficients two to three times greater than conventional organic CARs. Zirconium-containing resists showed only minor increases, attributed to zirconium's relatively small absorption cross-section compared to oxygen and carbon. Hafnium-based material displayed significantly higher absorption coefficients (about 9.0 μm⁻¹) due to hafnium's superior absorptivity.

The experimental setup for measuring absorption involved using the XIL-II beamline at the Swiss Light Source (13.5 nm EUV wavelength, >30 mW/cm² average flux). Photoresist samples were coated with 11-76 nm thickness and exposed under doses <20 mJ/cm². An AXUV100G silicon photodiode measured transmittance by calculating the ratio of measured photocurrent to reference photocurrent, with results compared against standard organic CARs.

## 3. Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresist Synthesis Techniques

### 3.1 Novel Synthesis Techniques for Metal Oxides and EUV Lithography Applications

#### 3.1.1 Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD)

ALD is a pivotal technology for semiconductor fabrication, enabling precise deposition of thin films crucial for sub-5 nm node devices in EUV lithography. By alternating exposures to inorganic and organic precursors, ALD achieves uniform, atomically precise coatings that enhance EUV absorption, mechanical strength, and etch resistance.

**Area-Selective Deposition (ASD)**

ASD is a strategic additive method that allows targeted growth of thin films on specific substrate regions, supporting applications such as selective epitaxial growth and cobalt capping of copper. Recent advancements expanded ASD application to post-development steps in EUV lithography, where selective deposition on patterned resists significantly reduces defect densities and improves pattern fidelity.

ASD capability to precisely target damaged sidewalls of EUV patterns directly addresses LER and LWR challenges. Studies demonstrated reductions in LWR and LER by approximately 26% and 35% respectively. ASD integration with EUV self-aligned double patterning showed progressive LER decrease as feature critical dimension reduces.

The application of ASD as post-lithography treatment has been pivotal in reducing defect densities, especially in thinner resists. Infusion of etch-resistant materials like TiO₂, selectively deposited on polymers such as poly(tert-butyl methacrylate), aids in hardening organic resists and facilitates construction of higher aspect ratio features crucial for advancing EUV capabilities.

**Vapor-Phase Infiltration (VPI)**

VPI, derived from ALD, hybridizes polymer-based materials by embedding inorganic elements into organic matrices. This technique infiltrates gaseous metal precursors into polymers during exposure, distinguishing itself from ALD through formation of inorganic-organic composites rather than sequential surface coating.

Various infiltration protocols including sequential infiltration synthesis (SIS) and sequential vapor infiltration (SVI) allow tailored improvements in LER and material robustness. The SIS process successfully reduced LER in EUV polymer-based resists by introducing trimethylaluminum (TMA) and water to form aluminum oxide networks within resist structure, improving etch resistance and structural integrity.

VPI practical applications in post-lithography treatments show substantial benefits in pattern performance and defect reduction. LER reductions and etch mask robustness have been significantly enhanced, allowing fabrication of higher aspect ratio features with improved depth control during etching. VPI has also been applied ex situ to pre-lithography coatings to generate hybrid inorganic-organic resist materials, significantly increasing EUV sensitivity and mechanical strength.

Notably, poly methyl methacrylate (PMMA) infiltrated with AlOx demonstrated increased etch resistance and process stability through higher cross-linking levels, crucial for advanced patterning techniques. VPI's ability to adapt and modify resist properties pre- and post-lithography highlights its versatility in addressing evolving EUVL technology needs.

**Molecular Atomic Layer Deposition (MALD)**

MALD extends ALD by enabling deposition of highly conformal, organometallic thin films directly onto substrates without spin-coating need. This technique offers atomic-scale precision in film thickness and uniformity, ideal for EUV photoresist fabrication. Unlike traditional ALD, MALD utilizes organic moieties along with ALD precursors to grow organic-containing films, facilitating development of hybrid inorganic-organic structures.

MALD potential for creating novel, dry EUV resists has been explored. Studies demonstrated that MALD can deposit and develop resists entirely in vapor phase, addressing challenges associated with conventional photoresist applications. Research using MALD to prepare hafnium-based hybrid films exhibited negative-tone resist characteristics due to selective dissolution behavior under electron beam exposure, demonstrating ability to achieve high-resolution patterning with critical exposure doses significantly lower than traditional methods.

Further research explored MALD films composed of TMA and hydroquinone, demonstrating utility in generating negative-tone patterns using low-energy electron exposure. These studies employed in situ Fourier-transform IR spectroscopy to elucidate chemical transformations within films, providing insights into mechanisms of solubility switching and cross-linking induced by electron interactions.

#### 3.1.2 Non-Aqueous Sol-Gel

Non-aqueous sol-gel techniques represent critical advancement in synthesizing metal oxide nanoparticles, especially significant for EUV lithography applications. This method utilizes organic solvents instead of water, enabling precise control over particle size, shape, and crystal structure at moderate temperatures, addressing challenges associated with traditional aqueous sol-gel processes.

By avoiding water, non-aqueous sol-gel reduces complexity and improves nanoparticle synthesis reproducibility while enabling formation of highly crystalline, uniformly shaped nanoparticles. This approach successfully synthesizes different metal oxide nanoparticles including IVB group oxides used in photoresist EUVL. The production of consistent, high-quality metal oxides demonstrates the growing importance of non-aqueous sol-gel techniques where particle quality and consistency significantly impact device performance and scalability.

Non-aqueous sol-gel chemistry is an advanced approach synthesizing metal oxides from organometallic precursors such as metal alkoxides and acetylacetonates, particularly useful in environments where water reactants cannot be used. This method primarily utilizes solvents supplying oxygen atoms necessary for metal oxide network formation.

The transformation involves key condensation reactions forming metal-oxygen-metal bonds:

- Through condensation of metal halides and metal alkoxides, metal oxides form alongside alkyl halide release, exemplified in anatase nanocrystal synthesis from titanium isopropoxide and titanium chloride.

- Ether elimination involves condensation of two metal alkoxides, forming metal-oxygen-metal bonds while releasing organic ether, applied in hafnium oxide nanoparticle synthesis.

- Ester elimination takes place between metal alkoxides and carboxylates, applied in zinc oxide, titania, and indium oxide synthesis.

- Amide elimination facilitates controlled nanostructure growth like titania nanorods through reaction of metal oleates with amines.

A more complex reaction pathway appears in BaTiO₃ nanoparticle synthesis in benzyl alcohol, where C-C coupling similar to the Guerret reaction leads to 4-phenyl-2-butanol formation. This mechanism excludes ether elimination instead involving C-C bond creation between isopropoxy ligand of titanium isopropoxide and benzyl alcohol, followed by hydroxyl group transfer and condensation leading to metal oxide formation.

These non-aqueous reactions underscore the crucial role of solvent and specific conditions in directing synthesis pathways, providing nuanced control over nanoparticle chemical structure and crystallinity.

**Surfactant-Directed Fabrication of Metal Oxide Nanoparticles**

Surfactant-controlled synthesis introduces key approaches for producing metal oxide nanoparticles with precise control over size, distribution, and morphology. This approach utilizes the hot-injection method where a precursor solution is injected into hot solvent containing surfactants. Surfactants prevent nanoparticle agglomeration by coating them, improve colloidal stability, and selectively adsorb to crystal faces to control nanoparticle growth dynamics and surface properties.

Such control has enabled synthesis of highly uniform nanoparticles including iron oxides and ferrites, with advanced control over particle size down to nanometer increments and specific shapes from spherical to cube-like structures. Key examples illustrate versatility and effectiveness of surfactant-directed fabrication:

- Iron oxide nanoparticles: achieving sizes from 6-13 nm in precise one-nanometer steps by manipulating surfactant concentrations and reaction conditions.

- Ferrite nanoparticles (MFe₂O₄): shape control (cube-like and polyhedron-shaped) achieved through non-hydrolytic reactions involving metal acetylacetonates, influenced by surfactant-to-iron ratios.

- Complex nanocrystal morphologies: examples include cone-like ZnO, titania nanorods, and MnO multipods. TiO₂, ZrO₂, and HfO₂ exhibit ability to create diverse nanostructures with controlled dimensions and distinctive morphologies using surfactant-mediated synthesis.

Despite the method's ability to produce diverse nanostructures, a significant challenge remains in the lack of universally applicable mechanistic principles that would facilitate rational synthesis strategy, highlighting the need for further exploration into organic reaction pathways and surfactant chemistry.

**Solvent-Directed Fabrication of Metal Oxide Nanoparticles**

Solvent-controlled synthesis represents a more straightforward and highly efficient method compared to surfactant-mediated approaches, primarily due to direct solvent involvement in the reaction process. This method typically employs minimal reactants—essentially the metal oxide precursor and common organic solvent—facilitating more straightforward characterization and chemical mechanism elucidation. Synthesis temperatures range from 50 to 200°C, significantly lower than hot-injection methods, contributing to improved product purity as surfactant-related impurities are absent.

Main advantages of solvent-controlled approaches include:

- Improved product purity: surfactant absence eliminates nanoparticle surface accessibility issues crucial for catalysis and sensing applications, reducing potential toxicity concerns from surface-adsorbed surfactants.

- Versatility of metal oxide precursors and solvents: the method accommodates wide ranges of metal oxide precursors like metal halides, acetates, acetylacetonates, and alkoxides, as well as various solvents from oxygen-containing organic solvents to 'inert' solvents like toluene, permitting tailored control over nanoparticle morphology and composition.

- Control over crystal growth and morphology: organic solvents and species formed during reaction act as capping agents, controlling crystal growth and influencing particle morphology, crucial for achieving anisotropic crystal growth and high crystallinity.

- Halide-free synthesis options: for applications where halide impurities are undesirable, solvent-controlled synthesis using non-halide precursors provides clean alternatives.

Examples of successful solvent-controlled syntheses include SnO₂ nanoparticles dispersible in tetrahydrofuran, demonstrating method efficiency in producing particles for high-tech applications. Additionally, BaTiO₃ nanocrystals synthesized using this approach exhibit high crystallinity crucial for piezoelectric and ferroelectric devices. Indium tin oxide nanoparticles with adjustable dopant concentrations and excellent electrical conductivity without additional annealing further highlight versatility and effectiveness in creating conductive materials.

Despite straightforward nature, achieving phase-pure multi-metal oxides remains challenging due to varying reactivities of different metal oxide precursors. However, benzyl alcohol solvents have demonstrated potential to adjust precursor reactivity, enabling synthesis of complex oxide nanoparticles with tailored properties.

### 3.2 Novel Inorganic Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresists and Synthesis Processes

Due to higher EUV radiation absorptivity, metal-containing resist materials are increasingly popular, offering superior sensitivity, durability, and etching resistance alongside enhanced resolution, pattern quality, and sensitivity performance. Prominent materials incorporate Hf, Zr, Zn, and Sn atoms, which significantly boost photoelectron generation under EUV radiation.

#### 3.2.1 Zinc-Based Inorganic Photoresists

The approach by Thakur et al. uses a denser zinc metallic oxo core as the inorganic component, aiming to improve material EUV absorption capabilities.

The synthesis process involves dissolving methacrylic acid (MAA) and Zn₄OC₂HF₃O₂₆ (or Zn₄OTFA₆, where TFA is trifluoroacetic acid) in chloroform, stirring at 40°C for 2.5 hours. After solvent evaporation, the oily residue is washed with toluene multiple times to eliminate excess MAA, yielding a white solid compound. For thin film preparation, a 2% (w/v) solution of Zn(MA)(TFA) in chloroform and propylene glycol methyl ether acetate (PGMEA) mixture (9:1 v/v) is filtered and sonicated, then spin-coated onto substrates at specific speeds for 30 seconds. Films designed for spectroscopic analyses are baked to remove residual solvents with thicknesses measured via atomic force microscopy and ellipsometry. These zinc-based oxo clusters with specific ligands show effective film formation and EUV sensitivity but experience solubility changes in thin film form due to structural adjustments.

To overcome traditional organic resists' low EUV absorption, researchers turned to metals with high photon absorption at 92 eV, like zinc. Studies on zinc-based oxo clusters showed significantly increased EUV absorption compared to conventional resists, offering promising approaches to enhance lithographic sensitivity and reduce errors.

Thakur et al. explored fluorine-rich zinc-based oxygen clusters with trifluoromethacrylate (TFMA) ligands to enhance EUV absorption, leveraging fluorine's significantly higher molar absorption cross-section compared to hydrogen and carbon. The process combines zinc-based oxo clusters with different organic ligands to enhance EUV absorption. This approach aims to improve lithography performance by promoting polymerization upon EUV exposure and incorporating more fluorine into resist material.

The synthesis method of Zn(TFMA) involves dissolving Zn(TFA) and trifluoromethacrylic acid (TFMAA) in acetonitrile, stirring, evaporating, and reprecipitating to remove excess acid, obtaining white solid. The solid is then dried and stored under nitrogen. Zn(TFMA)(MA)(TFA) synthesis involves using Zn(TFA)(TFMAA), TFMAA, and MAA in acetonitrile, following similar approach to Zn(TFMA) synthesis. Nuclear magnetic resonance analysis details ligand composition within the oxygen cluster. For application, the oxo cluster is formed into thin film by spin-coating in CHCl₃:PGMEA solution and baked. The silicon substrate is first treated with ozone to activate surface hydroxyl groups, then silanized using varieties of silanes including vapor-phase applied HMDS. Liquid-phase silanization involves mixing silane in ethanol and acetic acid solution, soaking the silicon substrate for 40 minutes with stirring, then cleaning, drying, and annealing at 130°C for 24 hours to enhance deposited material surface properties.

Two synthesis methods for zinc-based inorganic photoresists were developed to enhance EUV radiation absorption, aiming to improve lithography sensitivity and durability. Zn(MA)(TFA) preparation involves dissolution, stirring, and solvent evaporation steps, followed by film formation through spin-coating. Zn(TFMA)(MA)(TFA) synthesis introduces additional fluorine-containing compounds to increase EUV absorption by incorporating more fluorine. Additionally, this method includes ozone treatment and silanization of silicon substrate to improve film adhesion and stability. While both approaches aim to advance EUV lithography performance, fluorine compound introduction adds complexity and may affect material stability and polymerization rates despite potentially higher absorption efficiency.

#### 3.2.2 Tin-Oxygen Inorganic Photoresists

Tin-oxygen cage molecules can be considered as tin equivalents of silsesquioxane compounds composed primarily of silicon. Among them, hydrogen silsesquioxane (HSQ) is a well-known compound widely used for high-resolution patterning in electron beam and EUV lithography. Tin-oxygen cage molecules were first proposed by Puff and Reuter in 1989, and their use for EUV photoresist technology was explored by Cardineau et al. in 2014.

In the Cardineau et al. study, all chemical reactions were performed under nitrogen atmosphere. In initial attempts to synthesize organostannoic acid and dehydrate it to form the cluster, using phenyltin trichloride resulted only in insoluble white solid, likely tin(IV) oxide due to hydrolysis. Exploring various hydrolysis methods, they found that using ammonium hydroxide with organotin trichloride was the most successful. The resist formulation was created by dissolving the solid material in 2-butanone, filtered through 0.2 µm polytetrafluoroethylene polymer filter. These solutions were spin-coated onto 4-inch silicon wafers pre-coated with cross-linked hydroxyethyl methacrylate/methyl methacrylate copolymer adhesive film. Cardineau et al. created a 40 nm thick resist film by fine-tuning spin speed and formulation concentration. The films were then soft baked at 90°C, exposed to 13.5 nm radiation, and developed using isopropanol/water solution. Homolytic cleavage of the tin-carbon bond could occur during exposure.

Jarich et al.'s study examines UV and vacuum UV fragmentation and ionization pathways of tin-oxygen cage cations and monocationic complexes to understand fundamental photochemistry informing improved EUV photoresist material development. It reveals that counterion type significantly affects photoreactivity, suggesting that modifying counterions and organic groups attached to the tin-oxygen cage can optimize photoresist performance.

According to this research, reactions of tin-oxygen cage materials under UV and vacuum ultraviolet light, including homolytic cleavage of tin-carbon bonds and photoionization processes, are similar to those triggered by high-energy electrons in EUV lithography. These gas-phase experiments provide critical data and theoretical insights for optimizing and improving EUV photoresist material performance.

#### 3.2.3 IVB Group Inorganic-Based Photoresists

Foundational components for inorganic HfO₂ and ZrO₂ photoresists consist of a hafnium oxide or zirconium oxide core surrounded by organic ligands. The metal oxide nanoparticles are stabilized using carboxylic acids. To ensure these nanoparticles form consistent suspensions in aqueous or organic media, two distinct synthesis techniques were employed for hafnium oxide nanoparticles:

**Carboxylic-Acid-Ligand-Stabilized Hafnium Oxide Nanoparticles**

This synthesis involves dissolving hafnium isopropoxide in carboxylic acid surplus at 65°C, followed by gradual incorporation of water and carboxylic acid mixture. Upon 21 hours stirring completion, water addition facilitates product precipitation. This precipitate undergoes centrifugation at 8000× g for 5 minutes and subsequently dissolves in acetone and reprecipitates with water twice. After washing, the product is dried at 60°C in vacuum to yield white powder. To formulate photoresist solutions, appropriate quantities of obtained nanoparticle powder are incorporated into PGMEA, followed by adding photoactive compound and potential additives. This mixture is homogenized by stirring and ultrasonication to ensure uniformity. To ensure solution clarity and purity, particulate matter is eliminated through 0.2-micrometer filter membrane filtration.

**Ligand Exchange Process**

An alternative methodology alters nanoparticle surface characteristics by substituting surface-bound ligands with alternative carboxylic acids. In a standard synthesis procedure, 2.0 g of nanoparticles are first dispersed in PGMEA. A second carboxylic acid ligand is introduced into a small scintillation vial containing this dispersion. The mixture is heated to 130°C in a stirring oil bath until a transparent solution is achieved. Following this, water is added to solution to induce product precipitation, then subjected to several acetone washes to eliminate unbound acid. For photoresist solution formulation, accurate quantities of obtained precipitate are mixed into PGMEA. This mixture is homogenized by stirring and ultrasonication. To purge accumulated particulates or conglomerates, it is filtered through 0.2-micrometer pore membranes.

The same approach is used to prepare zirconium oxide nanoparticles starting from zirconium isopropoxide as precursor instead of hafnium isopropoxide.

The produced thin films can act as either positive- or negative-tone photoresists, depending on organic ligands used on nanoparticle surfaces during sol-gel processing, controlling thin film surface chemistry.

By employing hafnium oxide system combined with methacrylic acid as organic ligand (HfMAA), high-resolution negative-tone patterns can be achieved through incorporating either photoradical initiator or PAG as photoactive compound. Additionally, when extra post-exposure bake step is introduced along with aqueous tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAH) solution use as developer, the same films can be patterned in positive tone. Moreover, both positive- and negative-tone patterns can be obtained from zirconium methacrylate (ZrMAA) films by following identical procedures. By manipulating organic to inorganic content ratios, film density, etch resistance, and absorbance can be tailored accordingly.

Among transition metal oxides category, including titanium, zirconium, hafnium, cobalt, tin, and zinc, zirconium oxides emerge as the most viable resist material alternatives owing to their relatively low toxicity and cost. However, zirconium metal exhibits inferior EUV light absorption compared to other transition metal oxides, raising questions about ZrO₂ suitability as EUV photoresists. Nevertheless, when ZrO₂ coordinates with organic ligands, its EUV absorptivity approximates that of organic photoresists.

Initially investigated as photoresists, ZrO₂ nanoparticles coordinated to carboxylate ligands, such as methacrylate, encountered challenges due to nanoparticle size, leading to scum formation and limited resolution. Subsequently, Zr₆O₄OH₄methacrylate₁₂ clusters were employed for higher-resolution lithography. These clusters, approximately 1.7 nm in size, exhibit suitable characteristics for photoresist applications. Zr₆O₄ cluster films can be patterned with or without PAG at EUV doses ranging from 50 to 100 mJ/cm². Despite offering enhanced resolution, the lower sensitivity compared to ZrO₂ nanoparticles, in addition to low Zr₆O₄ solubility in development solvents, poses significant photoresist use challenges. Various strategies, including using different ligands and multiple ligands, have been explored to address these challenges.

Recently, a zirconium oxo cluster with methacrylate and acetate ligands was synthesized from diacetoxyzirconium (IV) oxide aqueous solution for EUV photoresist applications. This zirconium oxo cluster, featuring a zirconium core surrounded by methacrylate and acetate ligands, exhibits size comparable to Zr₆O₄OH₄methacrylate₁₂ clusters but demonstrates improved solubility in various organic solvents.

To synthesize the zirconium oxo cluster, 2.0 mL of methacrylic acid is introduced to 1.0 mL of zirconium acetate dihydrate aqueous solution under stirring, inducing precipitation. The formed precipitate is filtered using filter paper and subsequently dried under vacuum overnight.

For Zr₆O₄ cluster preparation, a solution comprising 2.0 g of methacrylic acid was combined with 1.73 g of zirconium butoxide solution (80% in n-butanol). The resulting mixture was stirred overnight. The ensuing precipitates are isolated via filtration and subjected to vacuum drying overnight. The acquired zirconium oxo cluster demonstrated both high sensitivity and high resolution as negative-tone photoresist.

## 4. Characterization Techniques for Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresists

### 4.1 Spectroscopic Analysis

Spectroscopy is the most widely used technique for characterizing EUV photoresist composition and optical properties. It provides detailed information about material chemical structure, elemental composition, and light interaction. Data collected from the spectrometer plays a crucial role in evaluating and optimizing photoresist lithographic process performance.

For metal-oxide-based photoresist development, precisely determining chemical reactions occurring upon EUV light exposure is crucial, influencing its sensitivity. The most common tool is X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), providing detailed elemental composition and chemical element states within the photoresist. XPS measures kinetic energy of electrons emitted from material surfaces when irradiated with X-rays. These electrons' binding energy provides information about present elements and their chemical states.

In addition to XPS, X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) is effective, collecting total electron yield data as a function of photon energy. This information helps determine metal atom oxidation states and interatomic distances in the photoresist, crucial for predicting photoresist behavior during exposure and development. Studies on tin-oxide-based photoresist chemical mechanisms utilized instrumentation systems consisting of vacuum chambers coupled to ambient-pressure gas cells by differentially pumped electron spectrometers.

Another spectroscopy technique characterizing metal-oxide-based photoresists is infrared nanospectroscopy (nano-IR). It is a non-destructive method allowing quantitative and qualitative elemental composition with minimal photoresist film damage, the biggest advantage of this technology. However, incident IR light's long wavelength limits typical nano-IR instruments' spatial resolution. Therefore, nano-IR is usually combined with atomic force microscopy by integrating the IR beam onto the AFM probe. As a result, spatial resolution reaches nanometer ranges, making nano-IR an efficient technique for obtaining chemical data on photoresist films.

### 4.2 Microscopic Analysis

Microscopic methods are widely used for assessing pattern fidelity, LER, and other critical lithography process parameters. Microscopy plays a major role in metal-oxide-based photoresist characterization, since assessing photoresist behavior and contained material properties is essential to reaching highest possible semiconductor manufacturing efficiency and accuracy.

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is the primary imaging tool for evaluating photoresist patterns. This method provides high-resolution nanometer-scale data, making it very efficient for visualizing fine metal oxide photoresist pattern details. It can be used at different pattern transfer process phases to investigate patterned feature quality and accuracy, such as uniformity and smoothness of the photoresist layer. SEM can also obtain cross-sectional photoresist images, providing vertical structure details, thereby accelerating photoresist development iterative processes and increasing throughput.

A specific SEM type, critical dimension scanning electron microscopy (CD-SEM), is currently the semiconductor industry standard tool for precise LER and LWR measurement. CD-SEM helps control lithography processes and allows making process parameter adjustments if deviations are detected. Moreover, it provides consistent measurement repeatability at specific device points.

Other microscopic methods commonly used for metal-oxide-based photoresist evaluation include transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM). The latter technique is ideal for material surface topography imaging. It is often used for post-exposure analysis to understand chemical changes and sensitivity trends in metal oxide photoresists.

## 5. Performance Evaluation of Metal-Oxide-Based Photoresists

### 5.1 LWR Results and Spectroscopic Analyses of Zinc-Based Photoresist

Thakur et al.'s study investigated fluorine-rich zinc-based oxygen clusters with TFMA ligands to improve EUV absorption and provided thorough Zn(MA)(TFA) photoresist EUV lithography performance evaluation. The ZnMATFA resist effectively created dense line/space patterns with half-pitches ranging from 22 to 50 nm, requiring low exposure dose around 20 mJ/cm², meeting current EUV resist standards. Pattern transfer was clean, with no scumming observed between lines. The aspect ratios were 1:3 for 50, 30, and 22 nm HPs, and about 1:2 for 40 nm HP. Most HP values met the industry requirement of LWR being less than 20% of the critical dimension. Achieving low LWR while using high-sensitivity resists is a significant challenge for EUV resists due to photon shot noise effects.

Spectroscopic analyses revealed Zn(MA)(TFA) photoresist chemical changes upon EUV exposure and demonstrated exceptional performance. FTIR spectroscopy showed significant carboxylate group peak reductions of TFA and MA ligands, particularly at 1676 cm⁻¹ and 1544 cm⁻¹, after EUV exposure. Additionally, C-F stretching vibration peaks decreased while aliphatic C-H stretching peaks increased, indicating TFA and MA ligand decarboxylation and C-F bond cleavage. UV-Vis absorption spectroscopy confirmed these changes, with the absorption band at 198 nm in unexposed material bleaching as EUV dose increased, reflecting double bond loss in MA ligands and cross-linked saturated carbon chain formation. XPS analysis revealed significant high-resolution C 1s, O 1s, and F 1s spectra changes, including Zn-F bond formation and carboxylate component reduction.

These spectroscopic results indicate that Zn(MA)(TFA) photoresist possesses high sensitivity, low dose requirements, high resolution, and excellent roughness control. Significant chemical reactions occurring at low EUV doses demonstrate its high sensitivity and low dose needs. MA ligand cross-linking forming saturated carbon chains confirms high-resolution capabilities. Additionally, Zn-F bond formation and other cross-linking reactions enhance pattern edge clarity and consistency, demonstrating excellent roughness control. These properties make Zn(MA)(TFA) photoresist a highly promising EUV lithography candidate.

### 5.2 Topographical and Chemical Analysis of Tin-Oxygen Inorganic Photoresists

The dual-tone property of the tin oxo cage BuSn₁₂O₁₄OH₆OH₂ photoresist was reported by Zhang et al. The TinOH photoresist was applied to pre-treated silicon wafers via spin-coating, creating a thin layer around 20 nm thick. This was followed by pre-bake to eliminate residual solvents. Samples then underwent various EUV or electron beam radiation doses. After exposure, samples underwent post-exposure bake at 150°C for 2 minutes, were developed in isopropanol and water mixture (2:1) for 30 seconds, rinsed for 30 seconds, and finally hard baked at 150°C for 1 minute to ensure complete solvent removal.

Topographical analysis using AFM and SEM revealed that low electron beam exposure doses (e.g., 50 µC/cm²) resulted in positive-tone patterns, with exposed areas completely removed. As exposure dose increased (e.g., 400 µC/cm² and 1000 µC/cm²), negative-tone patterns emerged, with unexposed areas showing reduced thickness, forming clear negative-tone patterns. Similar dual-tone behavior was observed with EUV exposure: low doses (e.g., 3.6 mJ/cm²) produced positive-tone patterns, while higher doses (e.g., 53.5 mJ/cm²) resulted in negative-tone patterns.

Chemical analysis using XPS showed significant carbon loss at high doses due to Sn-C bond cleavage, explaining negative-tone pattern formation. TGA results indicated that water loss during post-exposure baking at 150°C reduced unexposed film solubility, aiding positive to negative tone transition.

The evaluation highlights that TinOH photoresist exhibits dual-tone properties under varying EUV and electron beam exposure doses, enabling complex structure formation in a single photoresist layer. This dual-tone capability enhances patterning flexibility and precision. Additionally, TinOH shows high sensitivity to EUV and electron beam exposure, allowing clear pattern formation at low doses, crucial for high-resolution lithography. TGA and XPS analyses also indicate some thermal stability but high-dose chemical changes, such as carbon loss and material densification, affecting solubility and development. Overall, TinOH demonstrates excellent performance, including dual-tone properties, high sensitivity, and high resolution, making it a promising next-generation high-precision lithography candidate.

### 5.3 Analysis of IVB Group Inorganic-Based Photoresists

Markos's study showed that zirconium-based hybrid resists can achieve 30 nm resolution patterning with exceptionally high sensitivity. The process began with synthesis and preparation of the nanoparticles, where HfO₂ and ZrO₂ nanoparticles were synthesized through controlled chemical processes to ensure uniform sizes and proper dispersion characteristics. After exposure and development, developed patterns were analyzed using SEM, focusing on resolution, LER, and overall pattern fidelity parameters, providing detailed pattern quality insights.

The nanoparticle photoresists demonstrated high-resolution capabilities, producing patterns with line widths as small as 22 nm, essential for advanced semiconductor manufacturing. The patterns exhibited low LER, with roughness values below 3 nm, critical for ensuring semiconductor component accuracy and reliability. The photoresists showed high sensitivity to EUV radiation, achieving effective patterning at the specified 25 mJ/cm² dose, helping reduce exposure time and increase production efficiency. Additionally, the nanoparticle photoresists maintained integrity during etching process, showing excellent etching chemical resistance, crucial for preserving pattern fidelity during semiconductor fabrication. The spin-coating process resulted in highly uniform and consistent wafer coatings, ensuring reliable and repeatable patterning results across multiple wafers.

The inorganic HfO₂ and ZrO₂ nanoparticle photoresists reported by Markos et al. exhibit exceptional etch resistance, surpassing polymer resists by up to 25 times, and therefore enabling extremely thin film (<40 nm) processing. As a result, resolution limits below 20 nm can be achieved without encountering pattern collapse. Moreover, nanoparticles' small size (<5 nm) contributes to minimal LER and low EUV wavelength absorbance. Achieving high-resolution patterning (<30 nm) with remarkable sensitivity and low LER underscores these inorganic resists' efficacy, particularly in EUV lithography applications.

Further investigations reveal superior nanoparticle film etch resistance, attributed to thermal and chemical stability. Additionally, these films' hybrid composition allows precise absorbance control, optimizing lithographic performance. The successful ZrMAA film negative-tone patterning, utilizing record-low 4.2 mJ/cm² EUV dose, further highlights these materials' remarkable sensitivity in EUV lithography. Moreover, beyond enhanced etch resistance, these inorganic photoresists offer advantages including improved depth of focus and reduced LER compared to polymer alternatives. Additionally, successful pattern transfer into silicon substrates using SF₆/O₂ etching underscores these inorganic photoresists' efficacy in practical applications, further establishing their superiority over polymer counterparts.

In Kataoka et al.'s study about zirconium-oxo-cluster-based EUV photoresists, thermogravimetric analysis conducted in air aimed to characterize zirconium oxo and Zr₆O₄ clusters. Both cluster types exhibited stepwise mass loss between 100°C and 220°C, followed by thermal decomposition above 300°C. At 220°C, the zirconium oxo cluster demonstrated 47.1% mass loss, slightly less than the Zr₆O₄ cluster's 57.2%, suggesting smaller ligand fractions in the former. Powder X-ray diffraction pattern analysis revealed well-defined molecular crystals for Zr₆O₄ clusters, contrasting with lower crystallinity observed in zirconium oxo clusters. Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry measurements showed similar mass spectra for both clusters despite TGA and PXRD differences.

Dynamic light scattering measurements in methanol solution indicated comparable sizes for zirconium oxo (2.0 nm) and Zr₆O₄ clusters (1.8 nm), with slightly broader size distribution for the former. Notably, the Zr₆O₄ cluster size corroborated previous reports, while smaller zirconium oxo cluster size (compared to reported zirconium nanoparticles) suggests high-resolution photoresist patterning suitability.

The photoresist solubility was evaluated across various solvents (n-hexane, toluene, chloroform, PGMEA, butyl acetate, ethanol, and methanol). With n-hexane exception, all solvents formed transparent solutions with the zirconium oxo cluster, which exhibited rapid dissolution in many instances. Conversely, the Zr₆O₄ cluster demonstrated lower solubility in some selected solvents, requiring more dissolving time. Presumably, enhanced zirconium oxo cluster solubility can be attributed to its lack of crystallinity. In PGMEA, a common coating solution, zirconium oxo cluster solubility exceeded 20 wt%, while Zr₆O₄ cluster solubility remained below 1.4 wt%. Similarly, in butyl acetate, a prevalent EUV resist development solution, zirconium oxo cluster solubility surpassed 20 wt%, whereas Zr₆O₄ cluster solubility was below 1.5 wt%. Thus, markedly higher zirconium oxo cluster solubility across a broad solvent spectrum renders it highly suitable for photoresist application.

To evaluate zirconium oxo cluster photoresist efficacy, an open-frame exposure test was conducted, yielding normalized contrast curves characteristic of negative-tone photoresists. The zirconium oxo cluster film showed notably higher sensitivity compared to Zr₆O₄ cluster film, with approximately 3 mJ/cm² threshold dose for zirconium oxo cluster compared to approximately 5 mJ/cm² for Zr₆O₄ cluster. Additionally, the minimum zirconium oxo cluster full-height dose stood at approximately 13 mJ/cm², while for Zr₆O₄ cluster, it was approximately 25 mJ/cm². While Zr₆O₄ cluster sensitivity aligns with prior reports, zirconium oxo cluster sensitivity demonstrates comparable or superior performance relative to other reported photoresists.

Further investigation into zirconium clusters of 15 nm and 16 nm widths at pitches of 1:5 revealed noteworthy resolution capabilities. The zirconium oxo cluster resolved 15 nm width lines at 22 mJ/cm² dose, while Zr₆O₄ cluster achieved the same with 46 mJ/cm² dose. SEM imaging indicated satisfactory line width roughness for both clusters. Notably, zirconium oxo cluster superior sensitivity, as indicated by the contrast curve, was accompanied by observable scum or defect absence, a notable distinction from other nanoparticle photoresists. It is pertinent to mention that films underwent EUV light exposure in the absence of PAG. The zirconium oxo cluster exhibits sustained line width roughness when patterned at doses sufficiently low for industrial applications (22 mJ/cm²).

## 6. Applications and Future Prospects

### 6.1 Zinc-Based Inorganic Photoresist in Nanofabrication and 3D Printing

#### 6.1.1 Zinc-Based Photoresist for High-Resolution EUVL and Nanofabrication

Based on previous work on zirconium- and hafnium-based photoresists, Xu et al. from Ober's group developed zinc-based nanoparticles, achieving sub-15 nm patterns via EUV lithography. These zinc-based photoresists, akin to ZrO₂ and HfO₂ counterparts, feature a ZnO core with organic shell of acid and base ligands, offering fine particle size, solubility in spin-coating solvents, superior film formation, and effective patterning under UV and e-beam exposure. The zinc-based clusters facilitate nanostructure creation smaller than 15 nm, advancing nanofabrication limits to the cluster scale itself. This advancement aids contemporary microfabrication methods by enabling refined structure production compared to traditional polymer-based material achievements.

#### 6.1.2 Zinc-Based Photoresist for 3D Printing

Zinc-based photoresists share similarities with toluic-acid-based alternatives, featuring small particle sizes, excellent spin-coating solubility, and effective patterning under UV and electron beam exposure. Two-photon polymerization utilizing focused laser beams and photoresist materials can produce three-dimensional structures. These structures' feature sizes depend on the focused laser spot diameter and photoresist sensitivity. Zinc-based photoresists, particularly those with carboxylic acid ligands, demonstrate notable performance in two-photon polymerization, producing fine three-dimensional structures with resolution approaching diffraction-limited dimensions.

## CONCLUSION

Metal-oxide-based photoresists represent significant advancement in EUVL technology, offering superior EUV light absorption, high etch resistance, and enhanced sensitivity compared to traditional organic polymer-based systems. Zinc-based, tin-oxygen, and IVB group (zirconium, hafnium) inorganic photoresists have demonstrated exceptional performance in achieving high-resolution patterning at doses meeting or exceeding industrial requirements.

Key achievements include:

- **Zinc-based photoresists** achieving 22-50 nm half-pitch patterns with low LWR and excellent roughness control, demonstrating clear pattern transfer without scumming.

- **Tin-oxygen photoresists** exhibiting unique dual-tone properties enabling diverse pattern generation from single resist layers, with high sensitivity across dose ranges.

- **IVB group photoresists** achieving record sensitivities (4.2 mJ/cm² for ZrMAA), 22 nm line resolution, and exceptional etch resistance surpassing polymer resists by 25 times.

Recent advances in synthesis techniques—including ALD, VPI, MALD, and non-aqueous sol-gel methods—have enabled precise control over nanoparticle size, composition, and surface properties, critical for optimizing lithographic performance.

Advanced characterization techniques employing spectroscopy and microscopy have elucidated chemical mechanisms underlying resist performance, particularly photoinduced decomposition, cross-linking reactions, and solubility changes.

Future research directions encompassing process parameter optimization, novel ligand and metal combination exploration, and environmental benefits assessment of inorganic versus organic photoresists are poised to further enhance resolution and patterning capabilities requisite for next-generation semiconductor devices.

The transition toward metal-oxide-based photoresists is not merely incremental; it represents fundamental shift in EUVL chemistry enabling the semiconductor industry to surpass current resolution limits while maintaining manufacturing efficiency. As technology nodes advance toward 3 nm and below, these inorganic materials will play increasingly critical roles in enabling next-generation integrated circuit fabrication.
